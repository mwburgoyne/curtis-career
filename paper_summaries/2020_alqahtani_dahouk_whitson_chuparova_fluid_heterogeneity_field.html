Impact of Fluid Heterogeneity on Tight Unconventional Well GOR Performance

Authors: Fahd Alqahtani, Mohamad Majzoub Dahouk, Curtis H. Whitson, Elena Chuparova (NTNU / Whitson AS)

Venue: Unconventional Resources Technology Conference, Austin, 20-22 July 2020

Paper number: URTeC-2545

DOI: 10.15530/urtec-2020-2545

Citations (OpenAlex): 1

Well-Box Heterogeneity Field History Match Non-Uniqueness Ensemble Forecasting Eagle Ford EUR Spread Daily vs Monthly Data Phase 4

Context

Field-application capstone of the Alqahtani-Whitson-Chuparova well-box heterogeneity series. The 2018 URTeC-2882502 paper laid out the parasequence framework; the 2020 SPE-200014 paper established the kmax/kmin < 10 intra-layer threshold and proved the fundamental history-match non-uniqueness theorem on synthetic data; this paper takes real Eagle Ford production data from one well, systematically generates non-unique two-layer heterogeneous history matches, quantifies the resulting EUR forecast spread, and delivers the ensemble-forecasting recommendation as the operational response.

Key Contributions

  • Four-step systematic method to generate two-layer heterogeneous history matches. Step 1: assign φ1, compute φ2 from the arithmetic-average constraint. Step 2: estimate layer permeabilities from the Cantisano 2013 k-φ3 correlation. Step 3: estimate layer initial solution GORs to match the initial well producing GOR. Step 4: apply near-unity permeability scaling factors to reproduce single-layer kavg and pwf(t). Constrains the two-layer search space to cases that always match early-time producing GOR while preserving the reference single-layer HM quality.
  • Field-validated non-uniqueness. Eight two-layer heterogeneous models on a real Eagle Ford well all match observed oil rate, gas rate, OGR, and BHP. Cases 1 and 2 have identical petrophysical heterogeneity (k, φ) for both layers but radically different layer initial solution GORs (3000 / 5400 vs 8000 / 4500 scf/STB) - concrete field realisation of the non-uniqueness theorem proved synthetically in SPE-200014.
  • 10-year EUR forecast divergence despite equivalent history match. Good HM cases forecast ~85,000 STB 10-year EUR (similar to single-layer reference); over-predicting cases forecast 5-25k STB more (10-30% spread). FBHP endpoint choice (1325 vs 500 psia) has surprisingly small effect on 10-year oil EUR. The non-uniqueness manifests directly as EUR uncertainty even with a good HM fit - the fitting metric does not capture the forecast spread.
  • Monthly-public-data vs daily-metered-data GOR ratio 0.6-1.5 across 30 Eagle Ford wells. Public monthly production data requires a multiplication factor as extreme as 0.6-1.5 (varying per-well and with time) to match the daily-metered individual-well GOR. Industry-standard public-data history-matching workflows that take monthly GOR at face value inherit a 30-50% systematic bias before any modelling decision is made. Operational caveat with broad scope.
  • Ensemble-forecasting recommendation. The operational answer to the non-uniqueness theorem: history-match with a range of plausible GOR fluid initialisations constrained by early-time GOR performance, from uniform to layer-wise variation. The different history-matched models produce a distribution of oil and gas rate forecasts that quantitatively captures in-situ fluid-distribution uncertainty. Replaces "pick the best HM and forecast" with "carry the ensemble and report the spread".

Significance and Contribution

From theorem to practice. The non-uniqueness result was established synthetically in SPE-200014; this paper carries it through to a real well, a real ensemble of equally-good history matches, and a quantified EUR-spread number. The synthetic-to-field demonstration closes the loop and lets operators internalise the result with their own data.

The public-monthly-data caveat is independently load-bearing. The 0.6-1.5 monthly-vs-daily GOR ratio across 30 wells means that public-data benchmarking studies are inheriting a multiplicative bias before any modelling step. Operators with daily-metered data should use it; benchmarking studies based on public-monthly data should report the uncertainty floor that the ratio implies.

Ensemble forecasting as the practical response. The recommendation does not ask operators to solve the non-uniqueness inverse problem - it asks them to embrace it. Carry an ensemble of plausible fluid initialisations, report the forecast distribution, treat the spread as part of the answer rather than as a defect of the workflow. The framing aligns with the field-wide EOS philosophy (Younus 2019): commit rigour to the parts of the workflow that are constrainable, and report uncertainty for the parts that are not.
