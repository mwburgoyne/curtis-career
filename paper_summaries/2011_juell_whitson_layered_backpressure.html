Backpressure Equation for Layered Gas Reservoirs

Authors: Aleksander Oma Juell, Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU)

Venue: SPE Annual Technical Conference and Exhibition, Denver, 30 October - 2 November 2011

Paper number: SPE-146066

Citations: not catalogued

Backpressure Equation Layered Gas Reservoir Fetkovich Form PI-Weighted Pressure No-Crossflow Hugoton Field Well-Performance Monitoring Phase 4

Context

Aleksander Juell's NTNU M.Sc. thesis work with Curtis supervision. The paper derives a multi-layer backpressure equation (BPE) for wells producing from layered gas reservoirs with or without inter-layer communication. The deliverability problem in layered systems had typically been treated as requiring transient-superposition or convolution-integral machinery. Juell and Whitson show that the steady-state deliverability problem reduces exactly to the single-layer Fetkovich (1975) form, provided the layer constants and shut-in pressures are averaged correctly. Validated against numerical simulation and against 1967-2009 annual shut-in pressure and production data from the publicly-available Buf #3 well (API #3513900778) in the Oklahoma Guymon-Hugoton field - a three-layer no-crossflow system (Herrington, Krider, Winfield reservoirs from Fetkovich-Ebbs-Voelker 1994).

Key Contributions

  • Layered gas-reservoir BPE preserves the single-layer Fetkovich form with productivity-index-weighted pressure average. For N no-crossflow layers each obeying the Fetkovich single-layer BPE qgl = CRl(pcl2 - pw2):

    Total productivity index: CR = ∑l CRl

    PI-weighted average shut-in pressure squared: p̅c2 = ∑l (CRl pcl2) / CR

    Total rate: qg = CR (p̅c2 - pw2)

    The same form as the single-layer BPE. The PI-weighted average is the pressure recorded at the wellhead during shut-in because the layered total is zero at pw = p̅c. Backflow through the wellbore during shut-in between layers at different pressures is embedded in the derivation. Coupled with per-layer material balance, the framework also forecasts depletion. Validated against numerical simulation (matches within plotting symbol thickness across 20 years with multiple shut-in cycles) and against the Buf #3 field case (reproduces 1967-2009 annual tubinghead shut-in pressures; reproduces an independent 1989 layer-pressure test without refitting; fitted drainage radii 832/1119/1041 m for the three Hugoton layers).
  • Long-term BPE monitoring with consistent shut-in durations detects tubing and reservoir performance deterioration. A log-log plot of (p̅c2 - pt2) vs qg at the wellhead establishes a well-specific deliverability curve. Each annual test that falls below the line indicates performance deterioration (tubing plugging, near-well skin increase, water coning); points above the line indicate improvement. A +20 skin insult introduced in a numerical model after 10 years produces a clear shift below the established backpressure line. Operational requirements: consistent shut-in durations across tests (mixing durations creates spurious scatter); post-shut-in flow period of equal duration as the shut-in; record p̅c at the end of the shut-in; record qg and pt after the shut-in. The method is robust to partial or full layer communication (transmissibility 0 to 1 all on the same line) and to test-condition departures from normal operation - features the monitoring relies on because layer communication is rarely known a priori.
  • Validity scope: permeability above ~0.01 millidarcy, pressure-squared regime, no significant rate-dependent skin. Four explicit limits identified by sensitivity studies. Below ~0.01 millidarcy, transient effects dominate the deliverability response on annual-test timescales and the pseudo-steady-state assumption breaks. At high reservoir pressure (above ~300 bara dry gas) the pressure-squared form fails and pseudopressure must replace it. Wells with significant non-Darcy flow need a layered Forchheimer treatment that the authors could not successfully derive (acknowledged as an open problem). The pseudo-steady-state-on-shut-in-timescale assumption typically holds for k > 0.01 mD with 640-acre spacing.

Significance and Contribution

The multi-layer problem collapses to the single-layer form. Curtis's teaching pattern: the layered-well problem is often taught as requiring new machinery (transient superposition, convolution integrals); the Juell-Whitson result is that the steady-state deliverability problem reduces exactly to the single-layer form you already know, provided you compute the PI-weighted pressure as the "well-felt" shut-in pressure (not an arithmetic or pore-volume-weighted average). The familiar Fetkovich log-log plot remains the right tool, nothing lost.

The honest-limits move. The paper is unusual in explicitly cataloguing where its machinery fails: permeability floor, pressure-squared breakdown, non-Darcy open problem, pseudo-steady-state assumption. Many deliverability papers cheer-lead their methodology without quantifying failure modes. This pattern of explicit validity-regime cataloguing runs through Curtis's bibliography from Bøe-Skjæveland-Whitson 1989 onwards.

The Hugoton field validation. A 63-year (1946-2009) production record reproduced with three layer permeabilities and three drainage radii fitted to annual shut-in pressure history. The 1989 independent layer-pressure test is matched without refitting - the strongest test of the framework's predictive ability.
