Cyclic Shut-In Eliminates Liquid-Loading in Gas Wells

Authors: Curtis H. Whitson, Silvya Dewi Rahmawati, Aleksander Oma Juell (NTNU)

Venue: SPE/EAGE European Unconventional Resources Conference, Vienna, 20-22 March 2012

Paper number: SPE-153073

Citations (OpenAlex (constructed DOI)): 24

Context

The seminal Whitson-first-author paper on cyclic shut-in control (CSC) as a production strategy for low-permeability stimulated gas wells. The proposal is radical in its simplicity: whenever a well's rate falls to the Turner-like liquid-loading rate, a rate-controlled wellhead device automatically shuts the well in for a short period (~1 hour), then re-opens it. The cycle continues indefinitely until economic-rate cutoff. No downhole equipment, no artificial lift, no chemicals. Curtis's framing of the result emphasises its counter-intuitive nature: many shut-in periods would seem, intuitively, to represent "lost production", but the simulation demonstrates that short cyclic shut-ins preserve essentially the full ultimate recovery of a hypothetical continuously-unloaded ideal well. The paper is the conceptual foundation underneath the Knudsen-Foss-Whitson-Conn 2012 mixed-integer-linear-program (MILP) scheduling formulation and the Rahmawati-Whitson-Foss 2009 single-well constant-shut-in precursor.

Key Contributions

  • Cyclic shut-in control (CSC) as a liquid-loading-eliminating production strategy. The rule is binary: as soon as a well's surface rate falls to the liquid-loading rate qLL (per Turner et al. 1969, Coleman et al. 1991, Nosseir et al. 2000, Guo et al. 2006), a rate-controlled wellhead device shuts the well in for ~1 hour. During the shut-in, gas continues to flow into the wellbore and near-well region with some pressure buildup. When the well is re-opened, an enhanced transient gas rate follows, and the subsequent production period (during which rate is above qLL) is shorter than the previous period. The cycle repeats until economic-rate cutoff. Hardware requirement: only a rate-controlled wellhead shut-in device.
  • Short cyclic shut-ins (~1 hr) preserve approximately 100% of the recovery of the continuously-unloaded ideal. Recovery efficiency ERF = (RF - RFLL) / (RFCU - RFLL) is the fraction of the gap between the loaded-no-intervention baseline and the continuously-unloaded ideal that the strategy recovers. Vertical stimulated well: 1-hr shut-ins give ERF ≈ 100%; 1-day shut-ins give ~95%; 10-day shut-ins give ~85%. Horizontal multi-fractured (HMF) well (~10-5 to 10-3 millidarcy matrix permeability, 10 fractures, 1,524 m lateral): 1-hr and 1-day shut-ins give ~100%; even 30-day shut-ins remain above 95%. Physical mechanism: the wells are still producing while shut-in at the surface - gas continues to flow into the near-well region and charge the wellbore. When re-opened, the stored near-well gas is produced at an accelerated instantaneous rate. Most of the reservoir doesn't recognise the surface shut-in.
  • Permeability-bounded window where CSC recovery uplift is material. Vertical stimulated well: material uplift in k = 0.01 to 1 millidarcy. Below ~0.004 millidarcy, almost no gas is recoverable in 50 years even by the ideal case (CSC cannot help what the economic horizon won't reach). Above 1 millidarcy, the well depletes rapidly to ~90%+ before qLL is reached (mitigation unnecessary). HMF well: material uplift in k = 5 × 10-5 to 5 × 10-3 millidarcy with the same below-and-above mechanism. Peak uplift near k ≈ 0.03 millidarcy (vertical) and k ≈ 3 × 10-4 millidarcy (HMF). The permeability window serves as an operator-level screening criterion for whether to deploy the wellhead CSC device.
  • CSC works in layered no-crossflow systems with significant differential depletion. A two-layer demonstration (k1 = 0.785 millidarcy / h1 = 2 ft; k2 = 0.0523 millidarcy / h2 = 13 ft) shows that liquid loading onsets at 424 days with layer pressures 1,783 vs 2,216 psia (433 psia differential). Under CSC the two-layer cumulative-oil curve matches the continuously-unloaded ideal and does not degrade to the loaded baseline. Layer-specific pressures remain distinct - CSC preserves natural differential depletion rather than artificially homogenising it. Why: during the 1-hr surface shut-in, crossflow between layers through the wellbore is limited because the shut-in timescale is short and each layer's near-well region continues to charge independently from its own reservoir volume.

Significance and Contribution

The counter-intuitive teaching frame. Curtis presents the result explicitly as counter to engineering instinct, then demonstrates via simulation that the instinct is wrong. The paper's most-quoted passage:

The fact that cyclic shut-in control of a low-permeability stimulated well does not lose recovery or even delay the rate of recovery is a very important - albeit non-intuitive - observation. It suggests that such wells can be shut-in for a variety of reasons without any "downside".

And the framing of CSC as a necessary replacement, not an incremental improvement:

We believe that cyclic shut-in control will become an industry standard practice for shale gas wells, and should lead to a significant ultimate increase in worldwide gas reserves.

The basis for downstream optimisation. The Knudsen-Foss-Whitson-Conn 2012 ADCHEM12 paper takes the CSC concept and extends it to multi-well pad scheduling with target-rate tracking via MILP. Without the recovery-efficiency result in this paper, the MILP optimisation would be just another lost-production strategy. The 2012 SPE-153073 paper is the physics demonstration; the ADCHEM12 paper is the operational scheduling overlay.

The plunger-lift connection. The paper notes that plunger-lift is already a form of cyclic shut-in, but operators typically trigger it on buildup pressure or elapsed time rather than on liquid-loading rate. Switching the trigger to qLL aligns plunger-lift installations with the CSC framework - making plunger-lift-installed wells effectively run CSC. For wells with free-liquid accumulation, the Turner-like rate underestimates loading; redefine qLL via Gray-correlation pressure-drop excess over single-phase gas.
