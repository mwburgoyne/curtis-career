Tertiary-CO2 Flooding in a Composite Fractured-Chalk Reservoir

Authors: Mohsen Ghasemi, Wahyu Astutik (Petrostreamz), Seyyed Ali Alavian, Curtis H. Whitson (PERA / NTNU), L. Sigalas, D. Olsen (GEUS), V. S. Suicmez (Maersk Oil and Gas)

Venue: Journal of Petroleum Science and Engineering, Vol. 160, pp. 327-340 (received 2017, published 2018)

DOI: 10.1016/j.petrol.2017.10.044

Funding: Joint Chalk Research Consortium (JCR7)

Citations: not catalogued

Context

Third paper in the Ghasemi-Whitson-Alavian-Petrostreamz-Maersk North Sea chalk CO2-EOR series. Where the 2016 SPE-179550 CVD apparatus used a single whole-core for secondary-CO2 measurement, this 2017 JPSE paper extends to a composite geometry built from 6 Sigerslev outcrop chalk plugs stacked vertically with a centralised axial "fracture", run as a tertiary-CO2 experiment after waterflood. The composite geometry enables three new study dimensions impossible with a whole-core: capillary continuity between plugs (via removable filter paper / horizontal fractures), L/D scale effect, and inter-plug heterogeneity. EOS model is the pseudoised 10-component EOS10 from Ghasemi 2016; compositional simulation uses ECLIPSE 300 + Pipe-It automated history matching.

Key Contributions

  • Composite fractured-chalk core apparatus. Six Sigerslev outcrop chalk plugs (45 cm total length, 3.74 cm diameter) stacked vertically with a 0.6 cm centralised axial hole through all plugs representing the fracture. Qualitative filter paper between adjacent plugs preserves capillary continuity at reservoir conditions (258 bara, 110 °C). X-ray CT scanning selects homogeneous plugs and verifies axial-hole continuity. Hydrostatic core holder compresses the assembly axially for capillary contact between plug end-surfaces. Protocol extends the 2016 CVD: waterflood from the bottom of the fracture at 2 cm3/h until no further oil produced, shut-in 197 hours, then CO2 inject from the top of the fracture at 2 cm3/h.
  • Water blockage reduces tertiary-CO2 diffusion linearly with post-waterflood water saturation. Best-match diffusion coefficients for tertiary-CO2 after waterflood are 3.4-6x smaller than for secondary-CO2 from the 2016 STO experiment, due to high water saturation blocking direct CO2-oil contact in the matrix. Linear trend D/D0 vs Sw,WF provides a screening estimate for water-blockage effect. Two-stage D-switching is required for accurate history match across the full experiment time-line, paralleling the time-varying-D workaround used in the 2018 SPEREE follow-up.
  • Capillary continuity insignificant for tertiary-CO2 flooding when diffusion dominates. The methodological surprise. Adding 0.1 cm horizontal fractures between the 6 plugs (per Saidi 1987) to eliminate capillary continuity gives nearly identical waterflood and CO2-flood recovery curves as the capillary-continuous case. This contrasts with Saidi 1987, Horie 1990, and Ringen 2005 gas-oil drainage findings (where capillary continuity matters strongly) and aligns with Verlaan-Boerrigter 2006 and Suicmez 2011 miscible-condition findings (where it does not). Explained via Peclet number NPe = uL/D ≈ 0.01 in the matrix, confirming a diffusion-dominated regime where capillary forces are subordinate to molecular transport.
  • Sulfate-driven wettability alteration distinguishes seawater from sulfate-free formation water. Experiment-1C with seawater (initial 2,780 wt-ppm sulfate, injection 5,560 wt-ppm) gives ~70% waterflood oil recovery on North Sea Chalk Field (NSCF) composite chalk. Experiment-2C with sulfate-free synthetic formation water gives only 46-60% waterflood recovery. The difference is attributed to sulfate acting as wettability-alteration catalyst at reservoir conditions (110 °C) activating chalk anhydrite dissolution per Austad et al. 2015. Best-match Corey exponents and the imbibition switch saturation Swt differ significantly between experiments (nl = -4.7 / -30, Swt = 0.75 / 0.6). Operational implication for chalk CO2-EOR: higher waterflood recovery means higher post-waterflood water saturation, more water blockage, less tertiary-CO2 uplift.

Significance and Contribution

Composite geometry separates physics from artefacts. Curtis's teaching pattern in this paper: when an experimental result might be artefact of single-core geometry (capillary-continuity assumed because the rock is unbroken), build the apparatus that lets you remove that assumption and see whether the result survives. The composite-core finding that capillary continuity is insignificant for tertiary-CO2 in diffusion-dominated chalk is exactly the kind of result that single-whole-core experiments cannot test.

Two-mechanism diagnostic via Peclet number. The matrix Peclet NPe ≈ 0.01 certifies diffusion-dominated transport, which is why capillary continuity does not matter. The same diagnostic carried forward into the 2018 SPEREE follow-up, where it distinguishes diffusion from gravity-drainage and convection. This is the right way to anchor a counter-intuitive experimental result - cite the dimensionless number that explains it.

Sulfate as the load-bearing variable in chalk waterflood. The 70% vs 46-60% recovery swing across sulfate-bearing vs sulfate-free water is operationally important for North Sea chalk field developments where waterflood water quality is a design choice. Whitson's contribution to a multi-author paper like this is the EOS / simulation framework that lets the team quantify the downstream tertiary-CO2 consequences of upstream waterflood-water-quality decisions.
