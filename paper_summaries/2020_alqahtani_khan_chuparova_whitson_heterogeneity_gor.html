Impact of Heterogeneity on Producing GOR for Tight Unconventional Wells

Authors: Fahd Alqahtani, A. S. Khan, Elena Chuparova, Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU)

Venue: SPE Canada Unconventional Resources Conference (virtual), 2020

Paper number: SPE-200014-MS

DOI: 10.2118/200014-MS

Funding: King Saud University acknowledged

Citations (OpenAlex): 3

Context

Numerical-simulation follow-up to Whitson-Alqahtani-Chuparova 2018 URTeC-2882502, which laid out the well-box fluid-heterogeneity framework as a prospectus. The 2018 paper hypothesised parasequence-scale fluid and petrophysical heterogeneity; this 2020 paper tests the hypothesis with a two-layer non-communicating MFHW Eagle Ford simulation model and delivers quantitative thresholds and signatures. The same Alqahtani-Chuparova-Whitson team plus Khan adding simulation expertise.

Key Contributions

  • kmax/kmin < 10 intra-layer threshold for ignoring lateral petrophysical heterogeneity. When the maximum-to-minimum permeability ratio within each mapped parasequence is less than ~10, the layer can be modelled with arithmetic-average φ and k and yield Rp(t) behaviour essentially identical to a model honouring the full mapped heterogeneity. Consequence: an entire MFHW can be modelled with a single hydraulic-fracture symmetry element rather than a full-well-box heterogeneous map, giving "nearly three orders of magnitude" CPU reduction (30-60 minutes → seconds). Tested across kmax/kmin 1.4-45 using Aguilera 2013 linear and Cantisano 2013 cubic k-φ correlations; deviation from arithmetic-average becomes appreciable only at kmax/kmin > 10 for the cubic correlation. Caveat: threshold applies to intra-layer heterogeneity, not inter-layer.
  • Fluid-heterogeneity-only differential depletion as a distinct mechanism. Classical differential depletion (Fetkovich 1990, Thrasher 1995, McCoy 2000) arises from petrophysical contrast between layers. The novel observation here: equal-petrophysics layers with different solution GORs produce significant differential depletion purely from contrast in fluid viscosity and compressibility. Same petrophysics, different fluids, different drainage rate per layer.
  • Single-phase and two-phase GOR(t) signatures. Above saturation pressure (single-phase): monotonic decline from the mobility-weighted average of the two layers' initial solution GORs toward the lower-Rsi layer. Below saturation pressure (two-phase): complex non-monotonic behaviour including transient maxima and late-time GOR decreases as the higher-GOR layer stops contributing. The two signatures are diagnostic - well-level Rp(t) shape distinguishes the flow regime.
  • Individual-fracture Rp(t) variance vs commingled-well averaging. Individual hydraulic fractures in a heterogeneous well box produce widely varying Rp(t) curves (e.g. 3500-8000 scf/STB initial spread); the commingled well-header signal reproduces the arithmetic-average-well behaviour via statistical averaging that masks parasequence heterogeneity. The well header is not informative about per-fracture variability.
  • Fundamental non-uniqueness of LRS well Rp(t) history matching. Multiple combinations of petrophysical and fluid heterogeneity produce similar aggregated Rp(t). Well-level production data alone cannot constrain the subsurface layered architecture; vertical fluid sampling through multiple parasequences is the only direct constraint, but is impractical with current sampling technology and budgets. The non-uniqueness is the operational reason single-fluid-type EOS development with field-wide tuning (Younus 2019 URTeC-551) is the right framing rather than per-well per-layer EOS work.

Significance and Contribution

Quantifying when heterogeneity matters. The 2018 prospectus warned that LRS reservoirs were fluid-heterogeneous on parasequence scale; the 2020 paper says exactly when that heterogeneity does and does not show up in production data. The kmax/kmin < 10 threshold makes the single-symmetry-element model defensible for most Eagle Ford / Bakken layers, which directly enables the field-wide-EOS workflow at scale.

The arithmetic-average-well as the appropriate model resolution. The commingled-well Rp(t) being arithmetic-average is a useful negative result: do not waste effort trying to extract per-fracture or per-parasequence information from the well-header signal because the averaging has already destroyed it. Spend the modelling effort on the right layer of detail.

Non-uniqueness as engineering reality. Naming the inverse problem as fundamentally underdetermined - rather than just hard - is itself a contribution. The implication is that operators should not chase exact subsurface heterogeneity reconstruction from production-only data; they should use vertical fluid sampling (where economically justifiable) and treat the field-wide EOS as the load-bearing inference target. Pairs with the Carlsen 2020 surface-process triplet: the right place to spend rigour is the rate-data and EOS framework, not the per-fracture model resolution.
