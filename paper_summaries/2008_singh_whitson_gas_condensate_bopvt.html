Gas Condensate Pseudopressure in Layered Reservoirs

Authors: Kameshwar Singh, Curtis H. Whitson

Venue: Abu Dhabi International Petroleum Exhibition and Conference, 3–6 November 2008

Paper number: SPE-117930

DOI: 10.2118/117930-MS

Citations (OpenAlex): 5

Context

The Fevang-Whitson three-region gas-condensate pseudopressure method (SPE-30714, 1996; SPE-56476, 1999) had been the standard well-deliverability treatment for retrograde-gas wells for over a decade. Published verification covered single-layer radial models and single-layer Cartesian models. Operators applying the method in full-field studies with many numerical layers, strong permeability contrast, and capillary-number-dependent relative permeabilities had no published validation for the layered case. Barker's 2005 IPTC-10382 paper reported inconsistencies with the pseudopressure method that some interpreted as method-level failures. The community needed a settling paper.

Singh and Whitson provide it. They run the method through layered systems of increasing complexity using the commercial Eclipse 2007.2 EOS-based compositional simulator: a single-layer synthetic benchmark, a 16-layer Layered FW Model, the 10-layer Reservoir Beta, the 22-layer Reservoir Alpha (including a gas-injection case), and the 23-layer Reservoir Kappa with compositional grading from medium-rich to near-critical. Permeability spans 0.1 to 1000 md. Crossflow is tested both on and off, capillary-number modification of relative permeabilities is tested with and without non-Darcy flow. The paper positions itself as direct rebuttal to the Barker report - the failures are attributed to implementation issues in the simulator Barker used, not to method-level failures.

Key Contributions

  • First layered-reservoir verification of the Fevang-Whitson pseudopressure method. Single-layer permeability tested 0.1-15 md. Layered FW Model 16 layers 0.1-50 md. Beta 10 layers 0.1-90 md. Alpha 22 layers 0.1-1000 md. Kappa 23 layers 0.5-10 md with medium-rich-to-near-critical compositional grading. Across all of these, the coarse-grid model with pseudopressure reproduces the fine-grid radial benchmark.
  • Crossflow on or off does not matter. Non-communicating layer cases (explicit crossflow turned off) give coarse-grid pseudopressure results matching the fine-grid model within the same accuracy as communicating cases. The pseudopressure treatment is applied at the well-connection level and is insensitive to whether neighbouring layers exchange mass.
  • Capillary-number modification embedded via radial v(p). With krg(Nc) using the Whitson-Fevang miscibility-factor form and commercial-simulator defaults (n=0.65, αc0=10000), the capillary-number effect is included in the pseudopressure integral via the radial-flow v(p) embedding. Coarse-grid and fine-grid deliverability curves match essentially exactly.
  • Non-Darcy beta-flow handled. Adding non-Darcy treatment (Evans-Civan 1994 correlation, β = 6 × 108 to 7.5 × 109 m-1) alongside capillary-number modification gives fine-grid-versus-coarse-grid match within percent-level well-productivity differences.
  • Wide-range fluid composition per layer. Reservoir Kappa (23-layer compositional grading from medium-rich to near-critical, initial GOR around 1400 Sm3/Sm3) matches fine-grid behaviour in coarse-grid simulations with the pseudopressure option plus capillary-number effect. The method is robust across the compositional range.
  • Coarse-grid cell-size guidelines. The accuracy of coarse-grid pseudopressure depends on well-grid cell size. Quantitative guidelines from systematic sensitivity runs:
    • Lean gas condensate: Δx = Δy around 50-100 m
    • Rich gas condensate: Δx = Δy around 100-200 m
    • At gas rates 2-3x baseline (1.5-3 × 106 Sm3/d), halve the cell size
    • With capillary-number effect included, cell-size sensitivity relaxes by around 2x
    Lean condensates have sharper λt(p) transitions at the dewpoint so the pseudopressure integral concentrates in a narrow window near the wellbore. Rich condensates spread the integral over a wider pressure window and tolerate larger cells. Reservoir Beta at 0.7-0.85 × 106 Sm3/d (5 md) was adequate at 180 m; at 1 × 106 Sm3/d the cell had to drop to 90 m. With capillary-number effect on, 180 m and 90 m results were nearly identical.
  • By-connection pseudopressure for gas cycling and selective breakthrough. When layer-dependent GORs vary significantly (greater than 20-30%, typically after injected-gas breakthrough in gas cycling) the pseudopressure treatment must be applied at the individual well-connection level, not at a lumped well level. A broken-through layer produces at near-injection-gas GOR with rs → 0 and reduces to single-phase gas pseudopressure smoothly. A non-broken-through layer retains the full two-phase three-region treatment. A well-level lumped pseudopressure table cannot represent both regimes simultaneously because the CCE-on-produced-composition step uses the well-level mean composition which is neither one nor the other. Reservoir Alpha gas-injection case (22-layer sector, 1 producer + 4 corner injectors, 25% gas reinjection) verifies by-connection pseudopressure closely matching the fine-grid radial benchmark for both BHFP trajectory and gas plateau.
  • Simulator-selection criterion. Implementations of the pseudopressure method in reservoir simulators that do not support by-connection application cannot be used for gas-cycling evaluation. This is a specific simulator-selection criterion for unconventional or gas-cycling recovery projects.
  • Direct rebuttal of the Barker IPTC-10382 (2005) report. Barker reported pseudopressure-method failures. Singh and Whitson attribute these to simulator-specific implementation bugs rather than method-level limitations. The message: if your simulator reports pseudopressure does not work, the problem is in the simulator's implementation, not in the method.

Significance and Contribution

Closing the loop on the Fevang-Whitson method. Early pseudopressure work (Fevang 1995 PhD, Fevang-Whitson 1996 SPE-30714) worked the single-layer radial problem. The 1997 IBC and 1999 SPE-56476 papers generalised to any fluid and any PVT framework. This 2008 paper settled the practical question of whether the method actually works in the full-field reservoirs that practising engineers need to simulate. The answer is yes across every variation tested: permeability spread four orders of magnitude, crossflow on or off, capillary-number modification, non-Darcy flow, compositional grading, and gas injection at the connection level.

Practical cell-size guidance. The quantitative cell-size guidelines (lean 50-100 m, rich 100-200 m, halve at high rate, relax with capillary-number effect) are the kind of operational output that turns a methodology into a workflow. Reservoir engineers running full-field gas-condensate studies now have a defensible cell-size choice rather than picking on intuition.

By-connection pseudopressure for gas cycling. The extension of the well-level treatment to a per-connection treatment is required for gas-cycling studies and is the natural reading of the original Fevang-Whitson framework. SPE-117930 is where it is first written down and verified. Subsequent gas-cycling work in the Khuff carbonates and elsewhere uses the by-connection pattern directly.

The Singh-Whitson partnership matures. SPE-117930 is the second consecutive year of major Singh-Whitson output (2007 SPE-109596 on BO PVT, 2008 SPE-117930 on layered pseudopressure). The Singh collaboration runs through to 2025. StatoilHydro support, Øivind Fevang and Bob Mott acknowledged for technical discussions, situate the work in the Norwegian operator-and-consultant ecosystem around NTNU and PERA.
