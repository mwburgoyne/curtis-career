Context

Companion paper to Carlsen et al. 2019 SPE-196056 (Fluid Sampling in Tight Unconventionals). A Whitson AS plus EP Energy plus NTNU collaboration on gas huff-n-puff (HnP) process characterisation in the Eagle Ford. The paper synthesises three perspectives: fundamental phase behaviour via a proposed new PVT experiment, compositional reservoir simulation via a shattered-rock-volume (SRV) framework, and field-scale performance via a compositional-tracking methodology applied to an Eagle Ford pilot. Seven distinct contributions threaded through the workflow.

Key Contributions

  • HnP PVT experiment - Constant Volume Injection plus Constant Volume Depletion (CVI-CVD) hybrid. A novel PVT cell experiment that captures the cyclic build-up / draw-down structure of the HnP process. Step 1: inject gas at constant volume until pmax (mimics the huff). Step 2: single-stage CVD draw down until pmin (mimics the puff); unlike conventional CVD, the initial volume here can be two-phase. Step 3: repeat for the desired cycle count. Multi-stage CVD upgrade yields higher recovery because some production occurs above psat where EOR efficiency is higher. Distinct from conventional displacement-focused PVT experiments (slimtube, rising-bubble, vanishing-IFT) which are all predicated on a displacement process that HnP is not.
  • Three-scenario phase-behaviour classification with RMI invariance. Categorises HnP recovery behaviour by where pmin sits relative to psat and the first-contact minimum miscibility pressure MMPFC, yielding three fundamentally different recovery mechanisms: vaporisation-only, mixing-then-vaporisation, mixing-only. Recovery efficiency plotted as RFC6+ vs Relative Moles Injected (RMI) is invariant to the pressure-cycling interval Δp or the number of cycles. RMI is the right independent variable for cross-design comparison; absolute time or cycle count are not.
  • SRV multi-scale modelling workflow with rubble-size-dependent recovery. Three-step procedure for consistent HnP simulation: rubble-fracture single-porosity modelling at cm-to-m scale, upscale to a dual-porosity transfer term, then field / well-scale dual-porosity plus single-porosity composite. End-cycle recovery is inversely proportional to rubble size - smaller rubble means more matrix-fracture surface area means more mixing means more recovery. The workflow lets a small-scale numerical study calibrate the dual-porosity transfer for the field-scale model.
  • Dual-porosity vs single-porosity HnP compositional-signature contrast as field diagnostic. Single-porosity gives a piston-like response with no incremental uplift; dual-porosity with an SRV gives a mixing-driven response with substantial uplift. The two models differ strongly in produced GOR, API, C1, and C7+ trends - and those trends are themselves the diagnostic for which regime the field operates in. Field engineers can identify the operating regime by looking at production-composition trends rather than by argument.
  • HnP compositional-tracking methodology with two modes. EOS-based daily wellstream computation from readily-available production data. Mode 1 (sampling-available, Whitson-Sunjerga 2012): regresses wellstream composition to match all measured oil and gas properties via fg, C1-C6 slate, and C7+ MW. Mode 2 (production-only, Hoda-Whitson 2013): flashes the most-recent wellstream estimate and recombines to match the producing GOR. Basin-wide 35-component PR EOS C6-C30+.
  • Numerical-dispersion sign-reversal for HnP vs conventional gas EOR. The counter-intuitive numerical finding. Insufficient gridding in conventional miscible displacement gives artificially low recovery (numerical dispersion smears the miscible front). Insufficient gridding in HnP gives artificially high recovery (coarse-grid numerical mixing inflates SRV mixing). Reverses the conventional grid-sensitivity intuition - HnP engineers cannot transfer their displacement-flood grid-refinement habits.
  • Confinement concept plus Hammock plot diagnostic. An HnP control volume around the well and its neighbours frames the injection-gas loss question. The Hammock plot (cumulative injected gas vs average surface tubing pressure, across huff and puff cycles) exposes cycle-to-cycle hysteresis and improving pressure-build-up efficiency between cycles. For perfect confinement the HnP process behaves essentially as gas storage in a very-low-permeability system. The plot makes confinement a measurable quantity rather than an unspoken assumption.

Significance and Contribution

An experiment designed for the process. Curtis's teaching pattern: when an EOR process does not fit the standard PVT-experiment toolkit (slimtube, rising-bubble, vanishing-IFT all assume displacement), build the experiment that matches the process. The CVI-CVD hybrid captures HnP's cyclic structure in a PVT cell. The fact that conventional EOR-screening PVT experiments were used for HnP for years despite not matching the process is the kind of intellectual debt this paper resolves.

RMI as the right independent variable. The invariance of RFC6+ vs RMI across Δp and cycle count means that HnP recovery efficiency is fundamentally about how many moles you have injected, not how you have cycled them. This is a clean engineering result that simplifies design: optimise total moles injected, set cycling for operational convenience.

The numerical-dispersion reversal. Engineers who learned grid-refinement intuition on miscible-displacement floods will get HnP wrong by exactly the wrong sign if they transfer that intuition uncorrected. The paper's explicit warning is the kind of practitioner-protecting result that pays back the cost of reading the paper many times over.
