Scale Dependence of Diffusion in Naturally Fractured Reservoirs for CO2 Injection

Authors: Sayyed Ahmad Alavian, Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU / PERA)

Venue: 2010 SPE Improved Oil Recovery Symposium, Tulsa, 24-28 April 2010

Paper number: SPE-129666

DOI: 10.2118/129666-MS

Citations: 12

CO2 Injection Fractured Reservoir Molecular Diffusion Scale Dependence Haft Kel Matrix Block Size Lab Design Phase 3

Context

Field-scale companion to IPTC-10641 (Alavian-Whitson 2005 Haft Kel framework) and SPE-125362 (the 2009 laboratory reinterpretation). The two prior papers established the framework; this paper quantifies the diffusion contribution at field-scale matrix-block dimensions and contrasts it with laboratory dimensions. Two test systems: Haft Kel reservoir fluid (11-component SRK EOS tuned to Saidi 1987 PVT) in an 8-ft cube matrix block with 100-darcy fractures, vs a binary C1-nC5 synthetic oil (MMP1D ≈ 1,400 psia matched to Haft Kel) in a 0.2×0.2×2-ft laboratory-scale block. Diffusion coefficients from the extended Sigmund correlation (da Silva-Belery 1989). Eclipse 300 compositional simulator.

Key Contributions

  • Molecular diffusion dominates CO2 recovery rate in gravity-capillary-drainage fractured reservoirs, opposite to the viscous-dominated regime of SPE-125362. Two specific demonstrations:
    • Laboratory scale (0.2×0.2×2 ft, 1,000 psia below MMP): no-diffusion gives ~7% recovery in 10 days (piston-like gravity drainage); with-diffusion gives ~100% recovery in 3 days (CO2 penetrates the matrix and vaporises intermediates).
    • Field scale (8-ft cube, 1,000 psia below MMP): no-diffusion gives ~22% recovery in 5 years (plateaus); with-diffusion gives ~78% recovery in 10 years. At 1,500 psia above MMP, with-diffusion gives over 90% recovery in 2-10 years; no-diffusion still only partial recovery on 12,000-day timescale.
    Without diffusion, CO2 can only penetrate the matrix to the capillary threshold height and recovery plateaus. With diffusion, light and intermediate hydrocarbons below the threshold height are vaporised into the fracture gas and swept out.
  • CO2 fracture-replenishment rate controls the oil-vaporisation / gas-diffusion mechanism. Below a threshold injection rate, the fracture CO2 concentration drops as the gas mixes with vaporised hydrocarbons leaving the matrix; the compositional gradient driving diffusion collapses; recovery slows dramatically. Lab-scale: 16x drop in injection rate (0.5 to 0.03 cc/min) with diffusion degrades recovery from 100% to 15% in 10 days. Field-scale Haft Kel: 100x drop in injection rate (3 to 0.03 Mscf/D) degrades 10-year recovery from 78% to 62%. CO2 injection-rate design in naturally-fractured reservoirs must consider the fracture CO2 concentration at each matrix-block face, not just overall conformance.
  • Laboratory CO2-injection experiment design protocol for fractured reservoirs. Reservoir core or outcrop samples with 5-25 square-inch horizontal area and 10-30 inch height. Synthetic model oil constructed as a binary or ternary HC mixture whose MMP1D matches the reservoir oil's MMP1D (the model oil does not need to match viscosity or density in detail - only MMP1D, IFT, and oil-gas density difference). Core in a container with annular space representing the fracture; fracture width comparable to field scale; zero capillary pressure and 100-darcy permeability in the fracture to ensure gravity-capillary-dominated regime. Run CO2 injection at multiple pressures above and below MMP1D, and at multiple rates to characterise the fracture-replenishment sensitivity.
  • Matrix-block dimension scales recovery rate inversely while ultimate recovery converges above MMP. For Haft Kel with diffusion active: at 1,000 psia (below MMP), 8-, 16-, 35-ft cubes give ~78%, ~78%, ~68% ultimate recovery on different timescales. At 1,500 psia (above MMP), all three cubes converge to ~90% ultimate recovery, with rate scaling approximately as the inverse of block dimension. Above MMP, block-size heterogeneity is a rate-scheduling and economics problem, not an ultimate-reserves problem.

Significance and Contribution

The regime-classification framework. The 2009 SPE-125362 paper showed diffusion is negligible in viscous-dominated lab experiments. This 2010 paper shows diffusion is decisive in gravity-capillary-dominated lab and field experiments. The two papers together constitute the diffusion-regime framework: whether diffusion matters is a regime question, not a universal property. Use the capillary-threshold-height diagnostic to classify the regime, then include or exclude diffusion accordingly.

The lab-to-field warning. The scale-dependence demonstrated by contrasting 0.2-ft vs 8-35-ft matrix blocks warns against extrapolating lab-scale recovery time (days) to field-scale recovery time (years/decades). Diffusion slows with increasing block size because the diffusion path length grows faster than advective replenishment can keep up.

The Ekofisk and Haft Kel relevance. Both reservoirs were on the operator agenda for CO2-EOR through the 2000s and 2010s. The Alavian-Whitson three-paper sequence is the framework operators consulted for matrix-block-size sensitivity and injection-rate design.
