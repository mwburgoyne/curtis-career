Modeling CO2 Injection in a Fractured Chalk Experiment

Authors: Sayyed Ahmad Alavian, Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU / PERA)

Venue: 2009 SPE/EAGE Reservoir Characterization and Simulation Conference, Abu Dhabi, 19-21 October 2009

Paper number: SPE-125362

DOI: 10.2118/125362-MS

Citations: 10

CO2 Injection Fractured Chalk Karimaie Experiment Capillary Threshold Viscous vs Gravity Saturation-Based RF Diffusion Negligible Phase 3

Context

Model-based reinterpretation of the Karimaie (2007 PhD dissertation) and Karimaie-Torsæter (2008 SPE-113601) fractured-chalk gas-injection laboratory experiment. The experiment was designed as a low-interfacial-tension (IFT) gravity-drainage study on Faxe outcrop chalk (5 md matrix, 47% porosity, 19.6 cm core) but used Wood's-metal fracture-sealing technique to create the surrounding fracture annulus. Alavian and Whitson re-run the simulation in a 2-D r-z fine-grid Sensor compositional model with a 3-component C1-nC7-CO2 SRK EOS (parachor-tuned to 0.15 mN/m at 220 bar) and automated regression against measured cumulative oil production. The headline finding overturns the experimentalists' regime classification.

Key Contributions

  • Capillary-threshold-height diagnostic distinguishes gravity-drainage from viscous regimes. In a fractured-core gas-injection experiment, the displacement front position at late time is the diagnostic. If gravity drainage dominates, the front approaches but does not cross below the capillary threshold height hc = Pc,e / (Δρ g) (for Faxe chalk at 1.5 mN/m IFT, hc ≈ 2.7 cm). If viscous displacement dominates, the front sweeps below hc. The Karimaie experimental front propagates well below 2.7 cm throughout the equilibrium-gas injection - so gravity drainage is NOT the dominant mechanism. The Wood's-metal-filled fracture retained residual sealant after re-opening, giving fracture porosity ~93% (not 100%) and reduced fracture permeability (~26 md, regressed) - the experiment is viscous-dominated, not gravity-drainage-dominated as the experimentalists claimed.
  • Saturation-based recovery factor when matrix-fracture partition is uncertain. When cumulative oil produced includes an unknown contribution from fracture-oil displacement, the standard produced-oil RF is ambiguous. Alternative: RF = 1 - S̅o / So,ini from simulation, where S̅o is the volume-averaged matrix oil saturation. For Karimaie at end of CO2 injection, saturation-based RF is ~100% in the matrix while produced-oil RF is lower - the difference is oil that has left the matrix but not yet been accumulated as produced oil. Report both metrics alongside the model match.
  • Separator temperature is a first-order uncertainty source. Surface-volume measurement of produced oil is sensitive to separator stream temperature. CO2 has lower thermal conductivity than the equilibrium C1-nC7 gas, so the gas stream cools less efficiently through the ice-water bath condenser. The Karimaie experiment was matched only by regressing separator temperature in addition to fracture permeability: equilibrium-gas period at 5.8°C, CO2 period at 26.7-30°C - a 24°C step-change in separator T at the CO2-start transition explains the observed step-change in oil production. Surface-process uncertainty can masquerade as reservoir-process behaviour in history matching.
  • Molecular diffusion is negligible in viscous-dominated fractured-core CO2 experiments. Simulation runs with and without diffusion produce essentially identical core-average oil saturation profiles. Mechanism: advective flux through the matrix (from the fracture pressure gradient) sweeps compositional gradients before diffusion can act. Confirms Hoteit-Firoozabadi 2006 in a different geometry: diffusion matters in gravity-drainage-dominated systems, not in viscous-dominated ones. (Companion paper SPE-129666 by Alavian-Whitson 2010 covers the gravity-drainage-dominated case where diffusion is decisive.)

Significance and Contribution

The verify-the-regime-before-claiming-the-result discipline. Before claiming a gravity-drainage result, back-calculate the threshold height and check that the simulated and measured fronts stopped above it. The lesson generalises: in any experiment whose interpretation rests on being in a specific flow regime, build an independent regime-diagnostic check into the analysis. In this case the Wood's-metal initialisation procedure left sealant residue that the authors could not visually verify; only the threshold-height test exposed the regime failure.

Reported recovery factors are interpretation choices. In any experiment with fracture-matrix coupling or significant vaporisation, spell out which RF you are reporting and why. The practice of reporting both produced-oil RF and saturation-based RF alongside a simulation match is a disciplined form of error analysis.
