Modeling Gas-Condensate Well Deliverability

Authors: Øivind Fevang, Curtis H. Whitson

Venue: SPE Reservoir Engineering, November 1996, pp. 221–230

Paper number: SPE-30714-PA

DOI: 10.2118/30714-PA

Citations (OpenAlex): 364

LANDMARK PAPER Three-Region Model Pseudopressure Condensate Blockage Evinger-Muskat Well Deliverability Phase 3

Why it matters: The Fevang-Whitson three-region model is the practical industry standard for gas-condensate well-deliverability analysis. Every modern reservoir simulator's coarse-grid well treatment for condensate blockage, every analytical inflow-performance calculation for retrograde-gas wells, every pre-development deliverability forecast for a new gas-condensate field, traces back to this paper. The 1996 paper turned what had been a near-wellbore mystery (why does my gas-condensate well lose so much rate so quickly below the dewpoint?) into a structured three-region pseudopressure calculation that requires only producing GOR, PVT properties, and a single relative-permeability relationship. Thirty years on, it remains the conventional gas-condensate framework against which alternatives (the Carlsen et al. CVI-CVD framework for HnP, NRTA-based workflows) are compared.

Context

Gas-condensate well-deliverability loss had been an industry concern since the 1960s. Below the dewpoint, retrograde liquid forms near the wellbore, reduces gas relative permeability, and cuts well rate by factors of 2 to 10. By the early 1990s the qualitative picture was clear but the quantitative treatment was a mess. Simulators with no local grid refinement around wells dramatically overpredicted plateau period (six years instead of the correct two and a half for some lean condensates). Material-balance based pseudopressure methods using the CVD producing-GOR overestimated blockage loss. Practitioners ran fine-grid radial simulations as the only trustworthy approach, which made every field-development planning exercise computationally expensive.

The 1996 Fevang-Whitson paper builds directly on the 1994 SPE-28829 Fevang-Whitson sampling paper that established the reservoir-representative vs insitu-representative sample-quality hierarchy. The 1994 paper supplied the input - clean PVT compositions; the 1996 paper supplied the operational well-deliverability framework. The Evinger-Muskat 1942 pseudopressure idea is the analytical seed; what Fevang and Whitson did was identify the right physical regions, the right boundary conditions, and the right relative-permeability ratio variable to make the pseudopressure approach work for retrograde-gas wells.

Key Contributions

  • Three-region radial flow model. A gas-condensate well producing with BHFP below the dewpoint exhibits up to three distinct radial flow regions:
    • Region 1 (near-wellbore): Both gas and oil flow. The flowing composition (GOR) is constant throughout Region 1 and equals the producing wellstream composition. The dewpoint of the producing wellstream equals the reservoir pressure at the outer boundary of Region 1. This is the main source of deliverability loss because gas krg is reduced by condensate buildup. Region 1 saturation is whatever value makes the inflowing condensate mobile - no net accumulation.
    • Region 2 (condensate buildup): Only gas flows. Condensate accumulates with zero oil mobility. Saturations are closely approximated by the CVD liquid-dropout curve corrected for water saturation. Region 2 is largest at early times just after the reservoir pressure drops below the dewpoint and shrinks as Region 1 expands. More significant for lean condensates than for rich.
    • Region 3 (single-phase gas): Reservoir pressure exceeds the dewpoint. Original reservoir gas flowing. Exists only when the reservoir is still partially undersaturated.
  • Three-part pseudopressure rate equation. The Evinger-Muskat pseudopressure integral splits along the three regions:

    qg = C [∫pwfp*(krg/(Bgd μg) + (kro/(Bo μo))Rs) dp + ∫p*pd(krg/(Bgd μg)) dp + krg(Swi)∫pdpR(1/(Bgd μg)) dp]

    where C is a geometry constant, p* is the dewpoint of the producing wellstream, pd is the original dewpoint. The crucial claim: the pseudopressure integral, if evaluated properly, is independent of well geometry. All geometry effects (damage, fracture, horizontal trajectory) enter only through C and the skin s. Geometry independence is verified by fine-grid simulations for radial, vertically fractured, and horizontal wells.
  • Region 1 krg/kro is a direct function of pressure - no saturation needed. Because the flowing composition in Region 1 equals the producing wellstream, and producing GOR is observable:

    krg/kro(p) = [(Rp - Rs)/(1 - rsRp)] μgBgd/(μoBo)

    From the Evinger-Muskat framework krg and kro can each be expressed as functions of the ratio. So both relative permeabilities are known directly as functions of pressure in the Region 1 integral. Saturations never need to be calculated. This is what makes the method practical without fine-grid simulation.
  • Region 1 outer boundary p* from a surface measurement. The pressure where Region 1 ends equals the dewpoint of the producing wellstream. Using black-oil PVT, p* is where rs = 1/Rp. A surface GOR measurement defines a subsurface flow-region boundary directly. If p* > pR, only Region 1 exists.
  • krg = f(krg/kro) is the fundamental relative-permeability relationship. Not krg(Sg) and kro(Sg) separately. Simulating with two dramatically different saturation-based curve sets that share the same krg = f(krg/kro) relationship gives practically identical rate/time performance. The relevant krg/kro range is bounded: about 10 for rich condensates, 50 for lean. This is what should be measured in the laboratory; everything else is detail.
  • Critical oil saturation has no direct effect on deliverability. The perceived sensitivity to Soc in parametric correlations is actually sensitivity to the krg = f(krg/kro) curve. Changing Soc in a Corey-type formula changes the curve. Simulations with Soc = 0, 10, 20 per cent (modifying only the high-krg/kro end) give identical performance. Region 1 oil saturations are high; the low-saturation end is irrelevant. The corollary: sensitivity studies on Soc are misguided. Vary λ in the Corey equation instead.
  • Gas-oil IFT has negligible effect. Standard IFT-correction models (kr = F kr,imm + (1-F) kr,misc, F = (σ/σ*)n) show little effect on plateau period. Region 1 IFTs almost never fall below the 0.1-0.3 dyne/cm threshold once BHFP reaches minimum. The potential exception is rich, near-critical fluids in low-permeability reservoirs producing initially on decline - and even there the IFT effect is impossible to separate from other uncertainties. The paper's pointed observation: "Practically, it will be impossible to separate IFT effects from these other effects, and it will be difficult to 'sell' the optimistic effect of low IFT's to project economics when the IFT/relative permeability phenomenon is so poorly understood."
  • Coarse-grid pseudopressure method eliminates local grid refinement. Replace the standard pwf = pgrid - qg/J well treatment with a pseudopressure integral from pwf to pgrid. Pre-calculate Δpp(pwf, pgrid, Rp) as a 3D table during initialisation. The coarse-grid handles Region 2 pressure losses through surrounding cells while the pseudopressure table handles Region 1 within the well cell. Lean gas condensates and saturated fluids need smaller well-grid sizes; sensitivity runs at maximum expected rate determine the requirement.
  • CVD material-balance GOR overestimates blockage loss. Using Rp = 1/rs(pR) in the pseudopressure ignores Region 2. The actual produced gas is leaner than the CVD MB gas because Region 2 traps condensate without contributing it to the wellstream. For lean condensates with large Region 2 the CVD MB approach underestimates rate; for rich condensates after Region 1 fills the drainage it overestimates blockage.
  • Horizontal wells are strongly sensitive to kv/kh. Plateau period goes from 3 years at kv/kh = 0.1 to almost 10 years at kv/kh = 1 for the Rich Gas A example. Vertically fractured wells do not show this sensitivity. Horizontal-well gas-condensate developments should establish kv/kh with certainty before committing.

Significance and Contribution

The industry standard for thirty years. Every commercial reservoir simulator (Eclipse, CMG GEM, SENSOR, tNavigator) implements either the Fevang-Whitson pseudopressure method directly or a derivative blockage-skin treatment that approximates it. Every textbook chapter on gas-condensate well deliverability written since 1996 traces its structure to this paper. The 2000 SPE Phase Behavior Monograph chapter 7 carries the framework into the textbook. The 2006 SPE Handbook chapter on compositional simulation uses it as the reference well treatment.

The conceptual move. The key insight is that saturations are not the right variable in the near-wellbore. The producing GOR plus PVT plus the krg = f(krg/kro) curve fully determine the pseudopressure. Lab measurement programmes should focus on the 1 < krg/kro < 50 range. The shift from kr(S) thinking to krg(krg/kro) thinking is one of the clearer paradigm shifts in petroleum engineering since 1970.

Direct predecessor: the 1994 sampling paper. SPE-28829 (Fevang-Whitson 1994) supplied the conceptual framework for clean compositions; SPE-30714 supplied the operational well-deliverability framework that consumes those compositions. The pair are the entry-and-exit gates of Phase 3 of Curtis's career: sampling on the way in, well deliverability on the way out, both anchored on the gas-condensate problem.

Øivind Fevang's PhD and the start of the NTH gas-condensate group. SPE-30714 is the work of Øivind's NTH dissertation (defended 1995). It established Fevang as a senior collaborator and seeded the next round of NTH/Whitson AS gas-condensate work: the 1997 Whitson-Fevang miscibility-factor paper, the 1999 Fevang-Sævareid gas-condensate-bound water work, and the 2000 IBC paper with Yang. Mathias Carlsen et al.'s 2019 URTeC-539 CVI-CVD framework for HnP and Carlsen-Bowie-Dahouk-Mydland-Whitson-Yusra 2021 NRTA workflow position themselves explicitly as alternatives to the Fevang-Whitson three-region approach for unconventional applications - the framework is the reference point even where it is being superseded.

  • Fevang & Whitson (1994) SPE-28829 — the direct predecessor paper. Reservoir-representative sample framework and ECM compositions feed the deliverability calculation
  • Evinger & Muskat (1942) — the original pseudopressure idea for two-phase flow; the integral form the three-region split inherits
  • Muskat & Wyckoff (1937) and the classical radial steady-state framework — the geometry constant C setup
  • Coats (1985) black-oil vs compositional gas-condensate simulation work — the practical baseline the pseudopressure method outperforms
  • Whitson (1983) C7+ characterisation — the EOS-fluid framework feeding the PVT properties
  • Curtis's modified-black-oil (MBO) PVT framework for gas condensates — supplies Rs, rs, Bo, Bgd as functions of pressure
  • Whitson-Belery (1994) SPE-28000 compositional gradients — the depth-distributed PVT setup that informs the producing-GOR input

  • Laboratory kr measurement programmes designed to span 1 < krg/kro < 50 rather than the conventional kr(S) envelope
