An Equation for Fitting and Correlating Experimental Relative Permeability Ratio Data

Authors: Curtis H. Whitson (sole author)

Venue: Unpublished note, University of Trondheim (NTH)

DOI: none

Year: 1979

Citations: citation count not available (unpublished note)

Relative Permeability Knopp Extension Gas-Oil Ratio Curve Fitting Three-Region Model Phase 1

Context

Knopp (1965) had given a graphical construction for fitting water-oil relative permeability ratio data. The mid-range was log-linear in saturation; the tails were handled by reading deviation charts. For gas-oil systems no equivalent published construction existed, and the Corey/Johnson approach required residual saturations that the laboratory could not reliably measure. Trial-and-error fits to Johnson's charts produced "large deviations from chart values at low gas saturations" for Knopp's own data.

Curtis is 23, in his first year of teaching at NTH after Stanford, and writing a six-page note that does three things: turn Knopp's graphical construction into a computable equation, extend it from water-oil to gas-oil and gas-water, and give it a quantitative goodness-of-fit metric that lives in the same log-space as the correlation itself.

Key Contributions

  • Three-region equation for log Kg/Ko.

    log10(Kg/Ko) = A(Sg) + δ1(Sg) + δ2(Sg)

    with the mid-range A(Sg) = a Sg + b, the low-saturation tail δ1(Sg) = -10(c Sg + d), and the high-saturation tail δ2(Sg) = 10(e Sg + f). Six coefficients, each fit by least-squares linear regression in the coordinate system that makes the relevant piece linear. The same functional form covers Kg/Ko, Kw/Ko, and Kg/Kw.
  • Delta-function procedure for the tails. Define δ(Sg) = |log10(Kg/Ko)curve - log10(Kg/Ko)linear| as the absolute logarithmic deviation between the experimental curve and the mid-range linear extrapolation. A plot of log10δ vs Sg is a straight line in each tail region; least-squares regression on this plot gives the tail coefficients (c, d) and (e, f) directly. The construction replaces Knopp's Figs. 3C-3E with a computable equation.
  • Absolute conformance factor V±1.

    V±1 = antilog10[(1/n) Σ |log10 CRi|]

    where CRi is the ratio of correlation to curve at saturation point i. A reported value of 1.0295 means the correlation is 2.95% above or 2.91% below the smoothed curve on average. The metric is dimensionless and scale-free, which matches the quantity it scores: Kg/Ko ratios span several orders of magnitude. Fit in log-K space, score in log-K space.
  • No residual saturations as inputs. The equation deliberately omits SLr and Corey's Sm. Curtis argues residual saturations "are often difficult if not impossible to measure in the laboratory," so the correlation fits where the data exist and back-estimates residuals from the fitted equation if a downstream calculation requires them. The methodological move (fit what you can measure, back out what you cannot) recurs through Curtis's later PVT work, especially in CVD evaluation.
  • Averaged tail coefficients across Swi. Knopp had argued that c and d should vary with interstitial water saturation. Curtis found "a continuous and definable function could not be found" and uses averaged values (c-bar = -15.63, d-bar = 0.4133) with little loss of accuracy. A practical call: if the Swi dependence of the tail coefficients is below the noise floor of the underlying data, chasing it adds complexity without predictive value.

Significance and Contribution

Methodological signature. The 1979 relperm-ratio note shows the Whitson pattern at age 23, three years before SPE-12233. Take a piecewise problem, find the coordinate system in which each piece is linear, let regression do the work in each region, score the fit in the same space the fit lives in. The same move turns up in the C7+ characterisation (Riazi-Daubert in Tb-γ log-space), in the Hoffman log Kp vs F plot used in Whitson-Torp 1981, and in every later EOS-tuning workflow.

Pragmatic critique of Corey. Curtis is not saying Corey is wrong. He is saying Corey demands inputs (SLr, Sm) the laboratory cannot supply reliably. The rhetorical move (fit what is measurable, back-estimate the rest) is a recurring feature of the Whitson voice. It survives into the 1983 C7+ paper, the 1981 CVD evaluation, and the 2000 monograph.

What It Contributed To

  • Curtis's later "fit in the space the curve is linear" pedagogical pattern, which appears in SPE-12233 (1983), SPE-10067 (1981) Hoffman plot, and the 2000 monograph relative-permeability chapter
  • NTH petroleum engineering teaching infrastructure of the late 1970s
  • The dimensionless log-space conformance metric V±1, which fits any quantity that spans several orders of magnitude (typical of K-ratios, BIPs, and many EOS-derived properties)
