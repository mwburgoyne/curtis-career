Miscibility Variation in Compositionally Grading Reservoirs

Authors: Lars Høier, Curtis H. Whitson

Venue: SPE Annual Technical Conference and Exhibition, New Orleans, 27–30 September 1998

Paper number: SPE-49269

DOI: 10.2118/49269-MS

Citations (OpenAlex): 28

MMP-Depth Compositional Grading Zick Multicell C/V Mechanism Gas Cycling Slug Injection Phase 3

Context

By the late 1990s the compositional-grading framework (Whitson-Belery 1994 SPE-28000) and the Zick 1986 condensing/vaporising miscibility mechanism were both well established, but no one had joined them. Operators planning gas-injection projects on near-critical reservoirs faced two open questions. First, how does minimum miscibility pressure vary with depth when the in-situ composition is itself depth-dependent. Second, can gas cycling below the dewpoint in a depleted gas condensate ever be miscible. Industry convention said no - cycling below the dewpoint had to be immiscible because the vaporising drive needs reservoir pressure at or above the dewpoint.

Lars Høier was Curtis's NTNU PhD candidate working through the compositional-grading consequences across fluid zones. The 1998 paper is the first systematic walk-through of MMP-versus-depth for the three canonical structural archetypes (saturated slightly volatile oil, near-critical oil grading into very rich gas condensate, undersaturated volatile oil grading into rich gas condensate) and the first practical demonstration that enriched-gas slug injection can develop C/V miscibility far below the dewpoint of a depleted gas condensate.

Key Contributions

  • MMP-depth archetypes. For an oil zone, MMP always increases monotonically with depth (C7+ content rises downward). Vaporising-gas-drive MMP is always at or above the bubblepoint; condensing/vaporising MMP can be either side of pb depending on injection-gas enrichment. For a gas condensate zone, lean-gas VGD MMP exactly tracks the dewpoint gradient, but enriched-gas C/V MMP can drop significantly below the dewpoint. At a gas-oil contact with a saturated transition, MMP is discontinuous; at a near-critical transition MMP traverses the GOC smoothly. The classification reduces what looked like case-by-case intuition to a small set of archetypes.
  • Zick multicell algorithm with miscibility-distance criterion. Høier and Whitson validate Aaron Zick's multicell algorithm (1993 personal communication) for developed-miscibility MMP calculation. The algorithm tracks a physical distance-to-criticality parameter (e.g. oil-gas density difference) through a flash-cell sequence mimicking slimtube exchange, takes the historical minimum of that parameter, and extrapolates to infinite grid to give a dispersion-free MMP. Agreement with slimtube simulation is plus-minus 5-15 bar across 14 systems at 20-100x the speed. The historical-minimum treatment is essential because developed miscibility lives at an intermediate cell, not at the front and not at the inlet.
  • Critical enrichment E* and miscibility depth D*. For enriched-gas injection into a grading oil zone at fixed operating pressure, the minimum miscibility enrichment versus depth decomposes into two depth-independent quantities. E* is the enrichment level required to initiate the C/V mechanism (largely independent of depth in the oil zone, an observation first made by Zick). D* is the depth at which enrichment first becomes required to maintain miscibility at the fixed pressure. Above D* any injection gas works; at D* the required enrichment jumps from zero to E*; below D* MME increases monotonically with depth. The two-knob design (enrichment, pressure) reduces to a clean D*-E* decomposition.
  • Below-dewpoint miscible cycling. Høier and Whitson overturn the industry assumption that gas cycling below the dewpoint cannot be miscible. With injection-gas enrichment beyond E* and the right retrograde-condensate / injection-gas pair, the C/V MMP in the gas-condensate zone drops significantly below the dewpoint and can approach the bubblepoint of the retrograde condensate. Many gas condensate reservoirs previously dismissed as non-candidates for miscible cycling become viable. Cycling can be scheduled for operational flexibility (summer-only when LNG demand dips) rather than locked to full-pressure maintenance, and the pressure floor for miscibility drops, reducing compressor duty.
  • Enriched-gas slug as small as 0.10 PV. Continuous enriched-gas injection requires prohibitive NGL inventory. The paper shows a finite slug (around 0.10 PV) of enriched gas followed by a lean chase gas produces developed C/V miscibility practically identical to continuous injection, provided the slug exceeds the slug-chase dispersion size. If the chase breaks through and contacts the reservoir directly, miscibility is lost. The slug design converts cost-prohibitive cycling into an economically defensible scheme tied to seasonal NGL supply.
  • C/V MMP set by the retrograde condensate alone. For 1D slimtube displacement below the dewpoint, the C/V MMP of the gas-plus-retrograde-condensate system equals the C/V MMP of the retrograde condensate alone with the same injection gas. The reservoir gas phase has negligible effect on developed miscibility - only the condensate composition matters. The near-critical front locates at the liquid side of the phase envelope, so liquid composition dominates. Depletion history below the dewpoint matters indirectly by setting the retrograde-condensate composition at the start of injection.
  • Slimtube grid extrapolation fails for gas-condensate C/V. The standard RF1.2 versus N-1/2 linear extrapolation used for oil-system slimtubes does not work for gas-condensate C/V. Even at 3000 cells the front-scale dispersion is around 0.1 PV - comparable to the intrinsic C/V band width. Using 100-500 cells can produce an MMP estimate considerably higher than the true MMP. Multicell algorithms take over from slimtube simulation for gas-condensate C/V; slimtube is retained for validation only.

Significance and Contribution

Reopened a class of depleted gas condensate assets. The below-dewpoint C/V cycling result is the paper's most cited practical contribution. Before 1998, an operator looking at a depleted retrograde gas condensate had no miscible-recovery option in the standard playbook. After Høier-Whitson, the question became which retrograde condensate + injection-gas pairs develop the C/V mechanism, and at what enrichment level. The 0.10-PV slug design made the economics tractable. Subsequent enhanced-recovery work on depleted condensates (the Alavian-Whitson CO2 series IPTC-10641, SPE-125362, SPE-129666) builds on the Høier-Whitson framing of the gas-zone problem.

Joined two frameworks. Compositional grading (Whitson-Belery 1994) and developed miscibility (Zick 1986) had been separate threads. Høier-Whitson 1998 is the first paper to treat MMP as a depth-varying quantity in a graded reservoir, with the depth-dependence following systematically from fluid-zone and developed-miscibility-mechanism. The classification into archetypes is what made the framework usable in practice.

Set up the 2001 Theory and Practice paper. Høier's continuing PhD work produced the 2001 SPE-63085 compositional-grading paper as the matured theoretical companion to this 1998 application paper. The pair (1998 application, 2001 theory) plus the 2004 SPE-90379 Cheng-Høier-Whitson paper define the compositional-grading deliverable of Curtis's Phase 3 productivity peak.

Multicell over slimtube. The validation of the Zick multicell algorithm against slimtube as the reference method for developed-miscibility MMP is the practical lasting contribution. Slimtube simulation remains in use for ranking studies, but every modern commercial MMP package (PVTsim, Eclipse PVTi, CMG WinProp, whitson PVT) implements a multicell algorithm in the Zick style as the primary MMP calculator.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Zick (1986) SPE-15493 — the condensing/vaporising mechanism that revolutionised miscible-gas EOR
  • Zick (1993 personal communication to Whitson) — the multicell algorithm with distance-to-criticality criterion
  • Whitson & Belery (1994) SPE-28000 — isothermal gravity/chemical equilibrium framework for compositional grading; the depth-dependent input to MMP calculation
  • Hearn & Whitson (1995) SPE-29115 — demonstrated that VGD fails in saturated two-phase systems; this paper shows enriched-gas C/V succeeds in that regime
  • Whitson (1983) SPE-12233 C7+ characterisation — the heavy-end framework feeding the EOS predictions across the depth interval
  • Metcalfe-type multicell vaporisation models — the methodological antecedent the Zick algorithm replaces for C/V systems

What It Contributed To

  • Høier & Whitson (2001) SPE-63085 "Compositional Grading - Theory and Practice" — the matured theoretical companion paper covering thermal-diffusion uncertainty, GOC sensitivity, and field case histories
  • Cheng, Høier & Whitson (2004) SPE-90379 — the third paper in the Høier compositional-grading series
  • Whitson & Brule (2000) SPE Monograph "Phase Behavior", Chapter 8 — the gas-injection and miscibility chapter that consolidates the MMP-depth framework
  • Alavian-Whitson CO2-EOR series (IPTC-10641, SPE-125362, SPE-129666) — CO2 miscibility in depleted reservoirs follows the gas-zone branch of the MMP-depth framework
  • Multicell MMP algorithms as the default in commercial PVT packages, with slimtube relegated to validation
  • The enriched-gas slug-injection design pattern for depleted gas-condensate cycling projects
