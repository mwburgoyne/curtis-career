Generalised Pseudopressure Well Treatment in Reservoir Simulation

Authors: Curtis H. Whitson, Øivind Fevang

Venue: IBC Technical Services Conference, 1997, 12 pp.

Paper number: 1997 IBC Whitson-Fevang

Citations: Cited internally through the 2003 TIPM paper (29 OpenAlex) and the 2000 SPE Monograph; the IBC paper itself is not indexed

Generalised Pseudopressure Total-Mobility Capillary Number Coarse-Grid Well Treatment Black-Oil & EOS Phase 3

Context

By 1996 the Fevang-Whitson three-region gas-condensate pseudopressure method (SPE-30714) was the practical standard for coarse-grid well treatment of retrograde-gas wells. The 1997 IBC paper takes the next step. It generalises the pseudopressure formulation to any reservoir fluid system (single-phase gas, saturated and undersaturated gas condensate, single-phase oil, saturated volatile oil, dead oil) and to both black-oil and compositional equation-of-state formulations. It also handles injection wells, not just producers. And it proposes a capillary-number extension that embeds velocity and gas-oil IFT dependence directly inside the pseudopressure function, so coarse-grid simulations recover the near-wellbore Nc physics that fine-grid models otherwise need.

The paper is the methodological bridge between the gas-condensate-specific 1996 work and the 2003 Transport in Porous Media consolidation (Whitson, Fevang & Sævareid). The unifying observation is that the PVT calculation inside the pseudopressure integral is exactly a constant-composition expansion of the produced or injected composition. Once you see that, the same procedure works for every fluid type and every well direction.

Key Contributions

  • Total-mobility pseudopressure for any fluid system. Define mt(p) = ∫pminp λt(p) dp with λt = λg + λo. Surface gas and oil rates from a well grid-cell are then qg = βs C [mt(pG) - mt(pwf)] and qo = (1 - βs) C [mt(pG) - mt(pwf)] where βs = 1/(1 + Rp) in BO form or the mole fraction of separator gas in EOS form. The same equations apply from single-phase dry gas to saturated volatile oil and recover the Fevang-Whitson three-region integrand when only gas flows at the surface.
  • Black-oil and EOS unification through molar mobilities. The phase mobilities in BO form use kr/(μ B); the EOS form uses kr ρ/(μ M) giving molar mobilities. Both reduce to the same physical quantity at the surface separator, so the rate equation is fluid-formulation-agnostic. This is what lets a commercial simulator implement one pseudopressure routine that serves both PVT philosophies.
  • The PVT calculation is a CCE on the produced composition. The flowing composition in Region 1 equals the produced wellstream (or the injected wellstream in injection cases). Computing the pseudopressure table reduces to a constant-composition expansion at a handful of pressures from pG down to pmin. This is the unifying observation. One subroutine, one composition, one CCE per well cell per timestep.
  • Single-phase vs two-phase grid-cell branching. The pseudopressure table is built differently depending on whether the grid-cell is currently single-phase (Region 3-type, pG > upper saturation pressure) or two-phase (Region 1-type, pG = upper saturation pressure). The saturation-pressure discontinuity in λt is handled by linear extrapolation of two interior Region 1 values rather than evaluation at the bound. At the upper bound of a two-phase cell, the procedure deliberately does not use the cell-average λt known to the simulator for grid-to-grid flow - it extrapolates from the interior.
  • Injection-well treatment with previous-timestep composition. Injection follows the same procedure with bounds flipped (lower bound pG, upper bound up to the injection BHFP). The flowing composition input is the previous-timestep composition, not the nominally specified injection composition. This handles wellbore backflow between commingled grid-cells where the injected composition can range from dead oil to dry gas. An implicit composition would be more accurate but is computationally prohibitive in full-field simulation.
  • Capillary number from a radial-flow v(p) approximation. Velocity and gas-oil IFT effects on near-wellbore kr are embedded directly in m(p) via the steady-state Darcy radial-flow relation inverted to give r(p) and hence v(p). With v(p) known, Nc(p) = v(p) μg/σ follows, and Nc-dependent relative permeabilities are evaluated at each interior Region 1 pressure when the λt table is built. The radial-flow derivation is heuristic but conservative: linear-flow gradients are smaller, so radial gives a minimum Nc effect for the same rate.
  • The Whitson-Fevang miscibility factor applied at fixed krg/kro. A capillary-number transition function f = 1 - [(α Nc)n + 1]-1 blends the immiscible rock curve with the straight-line miscible end-points: krg = f krgI + (1-f) krgM. The defining feature is that the blend is evaluated at a fixed krg/kro value, not at a fixed saturation. This preserves the Evinger-Muskat Region 1 framework where both krg and kro are functions of the ratio. Fitting collapses from four parameter sets (Coats-style) to two: α and the exponent n ≈ -0.75.
  • Why coarse-grid simulators silently ignore Nc effects. The footnote pitfall: implementing Nc-dependent kr at the finite-difference level in a coarse-grid simulator gives identical results to running with the feature switched off. The cell-average velocity is far below the Region 1 near-wellbore velocity, so the simulator computes Nc ≈ 0 and reverts to the immiscible rock curves. The feature is active; the grid cannot see it. The m(p) formulation sidesteps this entirely because v(p) is reconstructed from the radial inflow relation at the well scale, not the cell scale.

Significance and Contribution

Generalisation from one fluid type to all. The 1996 SPE-30714 paper solved gas-condensate well deliverability. The 1997 IBC paper makes the same machinery work for volatile oils, black oils, dry gas, and injection wells, in either BO or EOS formulation. The total-mobility integrand and the CCE-on-produced-composition recipe are what every commercial simulator's coarse-grid well treatment now follows when it handles compositional well constraints without local grid refinement.

The Whitson-Fevang capillary-number form. The fixed-krg/kro interpolation between immiscible and straight-line miscible end-points is the methodological alternative to Coats's saturation-based IFT-threshold model. The choice trades fine-grid cell-by-cell applicability for a two-parameter fit instead of a four-parameter one. For coarse-grid well-performance calculation (the dominant industrial use case) this is the right trade. The form is recalibrated in the 1999 SCA-40899 paper with new Berea data and again in the 2003 TIPM paper with combined Heriot-Watt + Delft regression.

Phase 3 momentum. The 1997 IBC paper sits between SPE-30714 (1996) and the 2000 Phase Behavior monograph. Whitson and Fevang are working through the implications of the three-region model: how to treat any fluid (this paper), how to measure the underlying kr data without saturations (1999 SCA-40899), how to consolidate the theory (2003 TIPM with Sævareid). The IBC paper is the engineering generalisation; the SCA paper is the laboratory programme; the TIPM paper is the theorem and the design procedure.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Fevang & Whitson (1996) SPE-30714 — the three-region gas-condensate pseudopressure method that the 1997 paper generalises
  • Evinger & Muskat (1942) — the steady-state two-phase pseudopressure idea; the Region 1 krg/kro framework
  • Al-Hussainy, Ramey & Crawford (1966) — the single-phase pseudopressure form used in the refined v(p) calculation
  • Coats (1980) — the saturation-based IFT-threshold model the Whitson-Fevang form is an alternative to
  • Henderson et al. and ResLab gas-condensate kr(Nc) measurements — the fitting data for the original n ≈ -0.75 exponent
  • Peaceman (1978/1983) — the well-radius formulation underlying the steady-state well constant C

What It Contributed To

  • Sævareid, Whitson & Fevang (1999) SCA-40899 — experimental ResLab measurements refining the f(Nc) calibration to α = 104/√(kφ) with n = -0.64 on Berea
  • Whitson, Fevang & Sævareid (2003) TIPM — combined Heriot-Watt + Delft regression giving α = 2×104/√(kφ) with n = 0.7; the consolidated journal treatment
  • Whitson & Brule (2000) SPE Monograph "Phase Behavior", Chapter 7 — the textbook presentation of the generalised pseudopressure well treatment
  • Default coarse-grid pseudopressure well treatment for compositional reservoir simulation in commercial simulators (Eclipse, SENSOR, tNavigator) for any fluid type and any well direction
  • Subsequent gas-condensate and volatile-oil deliverability analyses that use a single procedure across the field-development lifecycle without changing PVT formulation
  • The fixed-krg/kro capillary-number interpolation is the basis for the Whitson-Fevang-Sævareid 2003 endpoint-scaling procedure for heterogeneous strata
