CGR Normalization - Convert Rates to a Common Surface Process

Authors: Mathias Lia Carlsen, Mohamad Majzoub Dahouk, A. Hoffmann, Curtis H. Whitson (Whitson AS)

Venue: SPE Canada Unconventional Resources Conference (virtual), 2020

Citations (OpenAlex): 1

Context

Third paper in the 2020 Whitson AS surface-process triplet (with IPTC-19596 compositional tracking and IPTC-19775 separator-oil shrinkage). Where the earlier two handle wellstream-composition prediction and stock-tank volumetric conversion at a given separator condition, this paper addresses the next layer: when separator pressure and temperature themselves drift over time, the resulting total CGR is still inconsistent with the fixed-surface-process assumption baked into black-oil tables used in reservoir simulation, RTA, PTA, and nodal analysis. The paper's claimed novelty is "a simple solution to a widely known, but under-addressed and overlooked problem, not earlier presented in the open literature."

Key Contributions

  • CGR normalization concept and 2-step procedure. Step 1: estimate the flowing wellstream composition using whichever of the four tiered methods the day's data supports (Hoda-Whitson 2013; Hoda-Hoffmann-Kuntadi 2017 two-ratio; Whitson-Sunjerga 2012; Carlsen et al. 2020 IPTC-19596 Method 3). Step 2: re-process the estimated wellstream through a single fixed common surface process (multi-stage flash, K-value process, or full process simulation in HYSYS / UNISIM). The output is a normalised CGR consistent across time and across wells. Four data requirements: tuned EOS, measured CGR, measured separator conditions, wellstream estimate. CGR normalisation runs after the SF/FF stock-tank conversion of IPTC-19775; it handles the residual error from drifting psep and Tsep that SF/FF alone cannot.
  • Fluid-type sensitivity framework. Sensitivity ratio δ = CGRmax / CGRmin across typical separator range (psep 50-1000 psia, Tsep 50-150 °F) decreases monotonically from ~10 for very lean gas condensates (solution CGR rs ~1 STB/MMscf) through ~2-4 for richer condensates, ~1.1 for volatile oils, ~1.0 for black oils. Practical rule: CGR normalisation matters for near-critical fluids and gas condensates, not for black oils or volatile oils. A field with swinging separator conditions and a lean gas-condensate well can report CGRs differing by up to a factor of ten with constant wellstream composition - an operationally large bias.
  • Gas Condensate Diagnostic Plot with CVD reference. Producing CGR plotted against flowing bottom-hole pressure (unconventional) or average reservoir pressure (conventional), overlaid against the CVD-released-gas CGR of the initial in-situ fluid. Without CGR normalisation, late-time forecasting errors reach up to 50% and the plot can show false indications of mobile condensate. With normalisation, the diagnostic separates real reservoir physics from operational surface-process noise.
  • CGR normalization as the mandatory first-step diagnostic before exotic physics. Operators proposing fluid heterogeneity, gas adsorption, or pore confinement to explain decreasing CGRs above saturation pressure in Eagle Ford or Duvernay shale wells must apply CGR normalisation first. Operational separator-condition swings can produce the same signature; ruling them out is the prerequisite for invoking nano-scale or compositional-grading explanations.
  • Field-wide / basin-wide common surface process recommendation. Companion to the field-wide EOS principle: a single common surface process should be defined for all wells in a field or basin and used for all reported surface rates (well tests, PVT, simulation) so that well-to-well and time-to-time comparisons are consistent. Pairs with the field-wide EOS to make the whole rate-reporting system self-consistent.

Significance and Contribution

Naming the surface-process inconsistency. Operators have lived with the surface-process drift problem for decades, usually compensating with hand-tuned constants. The paper names the inconsistency, frames it as a two-step rate correction, and ties it explicitly to the existing black-oil-table infrastructure rather than asking operators to throw away their tools. The operational hook is the four-tier wellstream-estimation table that meets each operator at whatever data density they actually have.

The "rule out the boring explanation first" instruction. Curtis's framing in the exotic-physics caveat is characteristic: before reaching for nano-confinement or selective gas adsorption, run the CGR-normalisation correction and see whether the anomaly disappears. The instruction is a Curtis hallmark - same pattern as the 2000 monograph PVT-data QC chapters: the first response to a surprising observation is a careful look at how the measurement was made.

Common surface process as basin-scale infrastructure. The field-wide-EOS recommendation from Younus 2019 and the common-surface-process recommendation in this paper together make the basin's rate-reporting system internally consistent. Same EOS, same surface process, same shrinkage / flash factor handling - everyone's well numbers comparable.
