Numerical RTA in Tight Unconventionals

Authors: Mathias Lia Carlsen, B. Bowie, Mohamad Majzoub Dahouk, Stian Mydland, Curtis H. Whitson, Ilina Yusra (Whitson AS / Apache Corporation)

Venue: SPE Annual Technical Conference and Exhibition, Dubai, 21-23 September 2021

Paper number: SPE-205884-MS

DOI: 10.2118/205884-MS

Citations (OpenAlex): 16

Numerical RTA LFP' Bti Equivalence Relationships Finite-Conductivity Fracture Contacted Pore Volume Dedicated to Mike Fetkovich Tight Unconventionals Phase 4

Context

Generalises the Bowie-Ewert 2020 URTeC-2967 numerically-assisted RTA workflow to (a) all fluid systems including gas condensates and two-phase saturated reservoirs, and (b) finite-conductivity fractures. Dedicated to Michael "Mike" Fetkovich, Curtis's long-standing material-balance-and-rate-transient interlocutor. Bowie carries over from the original Bowie-Ewert co-authorship at Apache; the Carlsen-Mydland-Dahouk-Whitson-Yusra core team is the same group that produced the 2020 surface-process triplet (IPTC-19596 / 19775 / SPE-199982), the HnP mechanisms / Second-Take pair (SPE-200396 / URTeC-2869), and the BOPVT-vs-COMP simulation paper (URTeC-2765).

Key Contributions

  • Modified Linear Flow Parameter LFP' = LFP × √φ with Bti generalisation. The classic LFP = 4 nf xf h √k (Wattenbarger 1998 for tight gas) is extended in two directions to make it the central parameter for all-fluid-system numerical RTA: (1) include porosity so cross-well φ changes are handled consistently; (2) generalise to all fluid systems via a saturation-weighted total formation volume factor Bti = (Soi + Sgi) / (Soi/Boi + Sgi/(Bgdi/rsi)). For dry gas, OGIP replaces OOIP via Bti → ∞.
  • Three fundamental LFP' / OOIP equivalence relationships. The theoretical backbone of NRTA: (1) same LFP' → same gas / oil / water surface rates during initial-acceleration linear flow regardless of OOIP; (2) same LFP' / OOIP ratio → same producing GOR and water cut at all times (IA and boundary-dominated), regardless of absolute LFP and OOIP values - well performance differs only by a constant multiplier; (3) same LFP' AND same OOIP → identical performance everywhere. Demonstrated with three-case worked examples.
  • Eight-step Numerical RTA workflow. Operational procedure: build a single-fracture symmetry-element "template model" with infinite OOIP; run with the actual well BHP profile pwf(t); compute the daily ratio r = qo,actual / qo,IA (or cumulative-ratio "cumulative LFP'" for noise reduction); multiply r by the template LFP' to get daily LFP'(t); pick representative LFP' from the early-time flat region; generate type curves at multiple smaller OOIP values; pick the OOIP curve best matching production data; resolve k and xf once L, nf, h, φ are assumed.
  • Finite-conductivity fracture Fcd consistency-conversion equation. When transitioning from the NRTA template model (single fracture, large permeability) to the history-matching realisation (multiple fractures, smaller permeability), Fcd must be recomputed via Fcd,2 = Fcd,1 × (k1 h1 nf,1) / (k2 h2 nf,2) × (β2 / β1). Worked example: Fcd,1 = 8 in the template model converts to Fcd,2 = 14 in the HM realisation.
  • Contacted pore volume Vp and gross rock volume VR as completion-effectiveness metrics. OOIP alone is misleading for cross-well completion-effectiveness comparison across very different fluid initialisations (an Swi = 100% reservoir has OOIP = 0 regardless of completion quality). Vp = HCPV / (1 - Swi) = OOIP / (1 - Swi) × Bti and VR = Vp / φ are fluid-independent metrics that can be regressed against completion design (proppant mass, fluid volume, cluster spacing) across a basin.

Significance and Contribution

The NRTA workflow that the unconventional cluster had been building toward. The 2020 surface-process triplet gave the operator the right rates; the BOPVT-vs-COMP paper made the simulation tractable; the well-box heterogeneity series gave the right reservoir model. This paper assembles the parts into a single rate-transient analysis methodology that handles all fluid systems and finite-conductivity fractures simultaneously - a generalisation that classical RTA, designed for single-phase oil and infinite-conductivity fractures, could not deliver.

The three equivalence relationships are pedagogical gold. Same LFP' means same rate during linear flow; same ratio means same GOR/WC trajectories; same both means identical. The hierarchy lets students and engineers reason about RTA outputs without solving the underlying problem each time. The Whitson-Brule SPE Monograph 2000 pattern of "name the principle, give the working tool" recurs here.

The completion-effectiveness metric correction. Naming Vp and VR as the right cross-well comparison axis - not OOIP - addresses a real industry confusion. Wells with high initial water saturation, gas-condensate wells, near-critical fluid wells all get pulled toward fair comparison when completion-team benchmarking uses contacted-pore-volume rather than initial-oil-in-place.
