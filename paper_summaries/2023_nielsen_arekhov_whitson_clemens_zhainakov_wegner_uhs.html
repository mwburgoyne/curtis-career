Fluid Modeling of Underground Hydrogen Storage in a Depleted Natural Gas Field

Context

Whitson AS / OMV E&P / HOT Microfluidics collaboration on a novel domain for the Whitson-group toolkit: underground hydrogen storage (UHS) in a depleted Austrian natural gas field. First-authored by Markus Hays Nielsen (Whitson AS), with Curtis third-listed; co-authors at OMV E&P (operator) and HOT Microfluidics (microfluidic PVT lab). Experimental and modelling campaign for hydrogen-hydrocarbon mixtures at UHS-relevant pressures (less than 200 bara) and temperatures (15-55 °C). The paper takes the same fluid-modelling discipline that Whitson AS applied to unconventional oil and gas through the 2010s and 2020s and brings it to bear on the energy-transition problem of storing hydrogen in depleted reservoirs.

Key Contributions

  • PR EOS + Peneloux volume-shift + Jaubert-group-contribution-BIP fluid model for H2-hydrocarbon mixtures. First complete engineering fluid model for H2-hydrocarbon UHS mixtures, parameterised to be usable directly in commercial reservoir / processing / transportation simulators. Complete parameter set (Tables A1, A2): temperature-independent volume shifts sH2 = -0.324 and sC1 = -0.107; BIPs H2-C1 = 0.362, H2-N2 = 0.263, H2-CO2 = 0.177, H2-n-C10 = 0.305. Achieves 0.19-0.54% RMS density match across four H2-enriched storage-gas mixtures (UGS-2 plus Mixtures B, C, D).
  • LBC viscosity tuning via H2 critical-temperature reduction. Adjusting Tc,H2 from -240.170 °C to -246.8 °C (a 7 °C decrease) in the LBC dilute-gas viscosity term improves pure-hydrogen dilute-gas viscosity prediction by 10-15% without measurable impact on volumetric predictions or hydrocarbon-mixture viscosity. UHS operating conditions are far from the H2 critical point so the adjusted Tc,H2 does not distort EOS Z-factor computation.
  • Anomalous near-constant gas viscosity in H2-hydrocarbon mixtures at high H2 content. Confirms via UGS-2 plus Mixtures B and C lab data (plus Kobayashi 2007 and Fokin 2011 literature) the empirical observation that gas viscosity at constant (p, T) does not decrease monotonically with increasing H2 mole fraction; instead it is approximately flat from 0% H2 up to a critical H2 content above 50 mol% (Fig D1a). Pragmatic implication: LBC predicts a monotonic trend (correct limit but wrong mid-range shape); default Pedersen is non-monotonic but wrong sign, overshooting in the opposite direction. Neither industry-default viscosity model is reliable mid-range; engineering judgement and per-project tuning are still required.
  • Three-channel-GC normalisation protocol for H2-hydrocarbon composition measurement. The three-detector GC (TCD1 calibrated for H2 only, TCD2 for other permanents N2 / CO2 / H2S, FID for hydrocarbons) requires FID-only normalisation (Normalisation 2) rather than global normalisation (Normalisation 1). TCD1 provides only H2 ratios, not absolute mass fractions, so normalising TCD values together with FID values propagates TCD calibration errors into reported FID amounts - up to 5 mass% error in H2 content from the wrong choice. An EOS-density-based QC loop (measure density, predict via PR EOS, iterate on H2 composition to match, compare against lab re-examination) confirms and corrects initial GC misassignments. Worked Mixture-A example: 19.82 mol% → 16.02 mol% H2 after the iterative QC correction.

Significance and Contribution

The Whitson toolkit applied to a new domain. UHS is one of the most-discussed energy-transition technologies and one of the worst-served by routine fluid modelling. Whitson-group publishes the first complete PR EOS + Peneloux + Jaubert-BIP parameter set with quantified density-match accuracy across four storage-gas mixtures. Operators planning UHS pilots now have a defensible engineering model rather than a literature-scattered patchwork.

Naming the anomalous mid-range H2 viscosity gap. Documenting that neither LBC (monotonic, wrong shape) nor Pedersen (non-monotonic, wrong sign) reproduces the mid-range viscosity behaviour is the kind of negative result that prevents downstream simulation errors. Operators using either model uncritically would build wrong well-deliverability tables.

The GC normalisation protocol as load-bearing lab-protocol contribution. Up to 5 mass% error in H2 content from a normalisation choice that GC technicians make routinely. The EOS-density QC loop is the kind of cross-validation that finds these errors before they propagate into simulator inputs. Mixture-A's 19.82 → 16.02 mol% correction is the worked example that justifies the protocol.

Curtis's late-career range. The paper extends the Whitson-group corpus from oil and natural gas into hydrogen storage. Same EOS framework, same lab-PVT QC discipline, same engineering-practice register, applied to a new energy-transition problem. The methodology built up across forty years carries over directly.
