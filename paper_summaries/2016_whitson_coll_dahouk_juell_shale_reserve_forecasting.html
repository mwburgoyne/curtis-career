Shale Reserve Forecasting: Model Consistency and Uncertainty

Authors: Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU), Carlos Coll (BG Group), Mohamad M. Dahouk, Aleksander Oma Juell (Petrostreamz)

Venue: SPE Europec featured at 78th EAGE Conference and Exhibition, Vienna, 30 May - 2 June 2016

Paper number: SPE-180140

Citations: not catalogued

Context

Curtis-first-author synthesis paper bridging three shale reserve-forecasting paradigms: Decline Curve Analysis (DCA, Arps), Rate-Time Analysis (RTA, dimensionless-solution-based), and Numerical History Matching (HM). The thesis is direct: DCA and RTA applied in isolation - fit to short rate-time histories without physical anchoring - produce inconsistent and often overly-optimistic estimated-ultimate-recovery (EUR) forecasts. The recommended practice is to use a history-matched numerical model as the anchor that supplies (i) the appropriate Arps b exponent, (ii) the infinite-acting (IA) to boundary-dominated (BD) transition time, (iii) the material-balance relation, and (iv) dimensionless-solution tables that RTA and simplified DCA can then apply to neighbouring wells that have not been history-matched. The paper threads four contributions through this consistency framework.

Key Contributions

  • Four-cycle rate-decline life-cycle for horizontal multi-fractured (HZMF) shale wells. Whitson's operational decomposition of HZMF production:
    • Cycle 1: IA flow with decreasing flowing bottomhole pressure (FBHP). Initial high-rate period (gas > 5-10 MMscf/D or oil > 1,000 STB/D) lasting months to years; FBHP decreases as the well produces against surface-facility constraints with fixed choke.
    • Cycle 2: IA flow with approximately constant FBHP. Multi-year period with sustained rates at near-constant FBHP driven by surface backpressure or artificial-lift targets; higher decline rate than Cycle 1, still IA linear or pseudoradial flow.
    • Cycle 3: Transition IA to BD with approximately constant FBHP. Not a single point but a gradual transition lasting months to years, caused by multiple no-flow boundaries and heterogeneities among individual hydraulic fracture (iHF) drainage volumes.
    • Cycle 4: BD flow with approximately constant FBHP. Drainage volume fully connected, material-balance depletion dominates; Arps b exponent and decline constant remain approximately constant.
    Each cycle maps to a different appropriate analysis method - Cycle 1 needs superposition or pressure normalisation or numerical modelling; Cycles 2-3 admit RTA with pseudopressure and pseudotime; Cycle 4 admits Fetkovich generalised DCA. Most HZMF wells operate primarily in Cycles 1-3 over the 30-year economic life and never reach Cycle 4 before economic cutoff.
  • HM-calibrates-RTA/DCA model-consistency framework. The paper's headline methodological recommendation. A history-matched numerical model is run once at constant rate to yield the dimensionless pressure solution pD(tD), or at constant FBHP to yield the dimensionless rate qD(tD) or qDd(tDd). These dimensionless solutions become transferable to neighbouring non-HM'd wells within the same geological sub-set. HM also calibrates the Arps b exponent, the IA-to-BD transition time, and the material-balance relation pR(Qp). The explicit answer to industry practice of fitting Arps to recent 1-3 year data without physical basis is: don't.
  • Threshold fracture conductivity kf wf > 1000 k xf for nano-darcy HZMF wells. Re-interpretation of the Golan-Whitson 1986 conventional rule of kf wf > 1000 md-ft. In nano-darcy rock the dimensionless conductivity criterion (FcD > 50 for "near-infinite") overstates requirements: a well with k = 100 nd and xf = 250 ft needs only kf wf > 25 md-ft for infinite-conductivity behaviour; k = 20 nd and xf = 500 ft needs kf wf > 10 md-ft. Conversely, conductivities 10-25 md-ft (conventionally considered "poor") behave as infinite-conductivity in nano-darcy environments. Important corrective for engineers transferring conventional fracture-design heuristics to shale.
  • Non-uniqueness JIA = Af √k in linear IA HZMF flow. During linear IA flow, the effective well fracture area Af = 2 Nf hf xf and the matrix permeability k cannot be differentiated from rate-time data alone. The worked example: three wells with Af = {1, 2, 5} × 106 ft2 and k = {100, 25, 4} nd respectively have identical linear IA decline because all have JIA = 107 ft2 nd0.5. This generalises Juell-Whitson 2013 SPE-166380 single-fracture xf √k non-uniqueness to the multi-fracture well level.

Significance and Contribution

Anchor the curve fit to physics. The headline message:

The history-matched numerical model is the anchor that supplies the Arps b exponent, the IA-to-BD transition time, the material-balance relation, and the dimensionless-solution tables that RTA and simplified DCA can then apply to neighbouring wells.

This is Curtis's response to a decade of operator-side DCA practice that fit Arps to recent data and called it a forecast. The right way to use DCA at scale is to use it as a calibrated extrapolator, not a free-fit. The HM model supplies the calibration.

Four-cycle decomposition as scaffold. Different cycles need different methods. Cycle 1 cannot be analysed with constant-FBHP DCA; Cycle 4 does not need numerical modelling. The decomposition is the scaffold on which method choice rests. Without it, practitioners are tempted to use a single method (usually Arps) across all four cycles, which is wrong everywhere.

The honesty about non-uniqueness. The JIA = Af √k generalisation extends the same theme from Juell-Whitson 2013: rate-time data cannot identify Af and k separately during linear IA flow. The right professional response is to acknowledge the bundle, parameterise the forecast in terms of JIA, and report the economic-forecast envelope - not to over-fit to a fictional point estimate. This discipline runs through Curtis's bibliography from Bøe-Skjæveland-Whitson 1989 onwards.
