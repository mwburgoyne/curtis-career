Compositional Variation in SAGD

Authors: Mohsen Ghasemi (Petrostreamz), Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU / PERA)

Venue: SPE Annual Technical Conference and Exhibition, Houston, 28-30 September 2015

Paper number: SPE-175022

Citations: not catalogued

SAGD Compositional Variation Vaporising-Condensing Mechanism Steam-Base K-Values Søreide-Whitson Viscosity Gradient Athabasca Bitumen Phase 4

Context

Fourth and final paper of the Ghasemi-Whitson SAGD series: 2011 SPE-147072 (black-oil-proxy framework), 2013 SPE-165416 (EOSSCN / EOS5 / EOS6 / LBC bitumen-with-solvent PVT), 2014 IPTC-18033 (solvent-SAGD numerical and IAM optimisation), and this 2015 paper consolidating compositional effects in pure SAGD without solvent co-injection. The work returns to the steam-only SAGD problem to ask a question deferred in the 2011 paper: does compositional variation matter for SAGD, and if so, when. Four contributions span steam-base K-value methodology, a novel second-order recovery mechanism, a vertical viscosity-gradient correlation for biodegraded bitumen, and a pseudo-fraction count sensitivity study. Same reservoir base case as IPTC-18033: 2D x-z half-symmetric Athabasca model, kh = 2,000 md, kv = 1,000 md, φ = 0.33, pi = 2,200 kPa, Ti = 10 °C, cold-oil viscosity 1.33 × 106 cp.

Key Contributions

  • Steam-base K-values via Søreide-Whitson 1992 αH2O correction with iterative ω-update. A workflow to predict K-values of bitumen fractions in a steam atmosphere (water as the vapour phase) at SAGD operating conditions, rather than the traditional CO2-base K-values commonly used in thermal simulators. The Søreide-Whitson 1992 αH2O0.5 = 1 + 0.453(1 - Tr,H2O + 0.0034(Tr,H2O-3-1)) replaces the default Peng-Robinson α for water, giving accurate water vapour-pressure predictions across 0-350 °C. Since most PR-EOS simulators accept α only through the acentric factor ω, the workflow iterates ω for water at each temperature until the PR-EOS-computed α matches the Søreide-Whitson value. Resulting steam-base K-values show sharp drops at the steam saturation temperature Ts = 219 °C at injection pressure 2,300 kPa. Operationally important finding: SAGD simulation using steam-base K-values produces oil-rate and recovery curves that match the traditional CO2-base K-value simulation, because thermal simulators operate the steam chamber at saturation where the two approaches converge.
  • Vaporising-Condensing Mechanism (VCM) as a novel second-order SAGD recovery mechanism. Light bitumen fractions (C7C20) vaporise at the steam-oil interface (high temperature) and condense at the edge oil-flow zone (lower temperature), enriching the interface with light fractions, reducing local oil viscosity, and lifting ultimate oil recovery. Magnitude scales strongly with heavy-oil API: ~1% recovery gain for base-case 8 °API bitumen, ~10% for 14 °API upgraded bitumen, ~12% for 18 °API. VCM is negligible for pure Athabasca but material for lighter heavy oils, and explains why field-observed SAGD performance sometimes exceeds the BO-proxy prediction for non-Athabasca settings.
  • Vertical viscosity-gradient correlation for biodegraded bitumen. A simple power-law form ln(μo/μo,top) = a · Dm with D = dimensionless depth and m = 0.772 base case (representing biodegradation severity, calibrated against measured Athabasca gradients). The gradient yields ~1% faster chamber growth and amplifies VCM effect by ~3.5% (gradient vs homogeneous) because lighter fractions concentrate at the top of the formation and reach critical viscosity earlier. Biodegradation produces these gradients in many real Athabasca settings; modelling them matters when forecast accuracy is the goal.
  • Bitumen pseudo-fraction count N = 1 / 2 / 5 sensitivity. Reducing bitumen to a single pseudo-component (N = 1) has negligible impact on pure-SAGD oil recovery (~72% at 6,000 days for all three) but does NOT capture VCM. Multi-fraction characterisation is required when VCM is material - lighter heavy oils, higher injection pressures, biodegraded reservoirs. The result gives the engineer a clear rule: skip multi-fraction characterisation for pure Athabasca SAGD if a fast forecast is needed; invest in 5+ fractions when VCM matters.

Significance and Contribution

The same physics, viewed correctly. Curtis's teaching pattern: K-values in a steam-driven process should be evaluated with the right vapour-phase composition. The Søreide-Whitson αH2O correction makes the EOS internally consistent for thermal applications. The operational finding that steam-base and CO2-base K-values produce the same SAGD answer is reassuring - thermal simulators can keep using CO2-base K-values without loss of fidelity, provided they remain at steam saturation.

When a mechanism is material vs negligible. The VCM result is calibrated by API gravity. For pure 8 °API Athabasca, VCM contributes ~1% and a single-pseudo-component model suffices. For 14-18 °API upgraded or naturally-lighter heavy oils, VCM contributes 10-12% and multi-fraction characterisation is essential. This kind of regime-by-regime guidance (rather than "always use 5 fractions") is the right way to set practitioner expectations.

Closing the Ghasemi-Whitson SAGD series. Four papers over five years built up the BO-proxy framework, the bitumen-with-solvent EOS, the solvent-SAGD coupled-system optimisation, and now the pure-SAGD compositional analysis. Subsequent Ghasemi-Whitson work pivots to CO2-EOR in Ekofisk-style chalk - the same EOS / IAM / Pipe-It methodology applied to a different recovery process.
