Two-Phase Pressure Test Analysis

Authors: A. Bøe (Rogaland Research), S. M. Skjæveland (Rogaland University), Curtis H. Whitson (Norwegian Institute of Technology)

Venue: SPE Formation Evaluation, December 1989, pp. 604-610

Paper number: SPE-10224

DOI: 10.2118/10224-PA

Citations (OpenAlex (DOI)): 85

Two-Phase Pseudopressure Well Testing Boltzmann Transform Solution-Gas Drive Gas Condensate dS/dp Phase 2

Context

A theoretical paper with a long gestation. The manuscript was first presented at the 1981 SPE ATCE in San Antonio, formally submitted November 1982, and published in SPE Formation Evaluation in December 1989. Curtis is a co-author across his Rogaland-Research and NTH affiliations during the years he was building Whitson Oil Inc. on the side. The paper provides what most two-phase well-test methods of the time were missing: a closed-form relation between oil saturation and pressure along the radial profile during the infinite-acting period, so that the pseudopressure integration is no longer history-dependent.

Key Contributions

  • A unique dS/dp relation for two-phase radial flow in the infinite-acting period. Combining the oil and gas diffusivity equations in Boltzmann coordinates yields:

    dS/dp = [(αα' - ȧα)(N/y) + (αb' - aβ')] / [(ȧα - α̇a)(N/y) + (aβ' - αḃ)]

    where a, α, b, β are constructed from the standard FVF, viscosity, and relative-permeability functions, primes denote saturation derivatives, dots denote pressure derivatives, and N/y at the wellbore eliminates the absolute permeability so that pseudopressure can be computed from surface-rate data alone. The equation reduces to known limits at short times (y → ∞) and long times (y → 0).
  • Producing GOR stabilises at a constant during infinite-acting drawdown. A direct consequence of the long-time limit. The producing GOR R = a/α reaches a fixed value within a few minutes of constant-rate drawdown start, then stays constant throughout the infinite-acting period. The stabilised level depends on rate and on the initial reservoir GOR. The result becomes an operational diagnostic for whether a drawdown test is actually in the infinite-acting regime - if the GOR is drifting, the test is either still in early transient or boundaries are being felt.
  • The validity-regime honesty. The paper explicitly catalogues three regimes:
    • Infinite-acting drawdown: liquid analogy works; a semilog plot of the wellbore pseudopressure gives the correct permeability.
    • Infinite-acting buildup from a drawdown: the wellbore S-p relation is still correct but the Horner plot does not yield the right slope. Reason: pseudopressure should integrate along the radial profile, but wellbore data alone cannot reconstruct the in-place S(r) profile.
    • Pseudosteady-state (PSS): Boltzmann transform fails; the dS/dp relation is invalid; conventional semilog interpretation cannot be applied. Raghavan's 1976 GOR-based method or Agarwal pseudotime is needed instead.

Significance and Contribution

The two-phase-pseudopressure closure. Without a closed-form S(p) relation, the multiphase pseudopressure integral is history-dependent and cannot be evaluated from wellbore pressure data alone. The Bøe-Skjæveland-Whitson Eq. 17 supplies the closure under one assumption (Boltzmann transform validity). This is the theoretical bedrock that lets two-phase well-test interpretation use the same semilog machinery that engineers had been using for single-phase liquid tests since the 1950s.

The producing-GOR-as-diagnostic insight. The constant-GOR result during infinite-acting flow becomes the foundation of the Fevang-Whitson 1996 three-region gas-condensate model. In that later paper the Region 1 framework relies on knowing the producing GOR from surface measurement; Bøe-Skjæveland-Whitson 1989 is the theoretical justification for why this quantity is well-defined and stable.

The honest-limits move. The paper is unusual in carefully cataloguing what its own machinery cannot do. Two of the three regimes (buildup and PSS) are where it fails, with a clear explanation and pointers to alternative methods. The discipline of bounding one's own framework is a Whitson signature.

What It Contributed To
