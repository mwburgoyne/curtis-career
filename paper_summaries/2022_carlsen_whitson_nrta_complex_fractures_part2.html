Context

Companion to URTeC-3718584 (Carlsen-Whitson 2022 Part 1, which outlines the underlying theory). This paper focuses on practical usage of fractional RTA theory applied to simulated and field data from the SPE data repository. Generalises the SPE-205884 numerical RTA workflow (with LFP' = LFP × √φ, all-fluid Bti, three equivalence relationships) from the classical case (δ = 0.5, evenly-spaced fractures) to the fractional case (any δ, uneven spacing) using the Acuña 2016 / 2020 fractal-network framework. Funded by the Numerical RTA JIP plus Innovation Norway plus the Norwegian Research Council.

Key Contributions

  • Acuña δ-parameter generalisation of LFP'. LFP' = A kδ φ1-δ = 4 nf h xf kδ φ1-δ, with δ ∈ [0, 0.5] measuring fracture-spacing heterogeneity (0 = pure fracture swarm, 0.5 = evenly-spaced classical case). Extracted from data via a power-law fit Δp / q = m t1-δ + b on the rate-normalised-pressure-vs-time log-log plot. Recovers the SPE-205884 classical workflow exactly at δ = 0.5.
  • Distribution-of-fragments reconstruction procedure. Closed-form equations convert (δ, Smin, Smax, m) into a fragment-size distribution {Si, ni} with ratio R = exp(ln(Smax/Smin) / (m-1)), fragment count ni = n1 (R(1-2δ)(i-1) - R(1-2δ)i), and n1 from mass balance on the total well length. Enables simulation of fracture-swarm networks via m independent symmetry-element models.
  • Arps b-parameter ↔ δ-parameter identity b = 1 / (1 - δ). Derived by equating the Arps hyperbolic rate decline qi (1 + b Di t)-1/b for large t to the fractional-RTA power-law q = (Δp / m) t-(1-δ) at constant BHP. Explains the Arps b ∈ (1, 2) routinely observed in unconventional wells after end-of-linear-flow (b=2 corresponds to δ = 0.5) as a transitional-flow fingerprint rather than a forecasting anomaly.
  • Classical-NRTA breakdown for transitional-flow wells. When δ = 0.5 is assumed but the well exhibits transitional flow over the entire or extended observed history, classical NRTA under-estimates both LFP and OOIP. Worked example on the Crow well (SPE data repository, 220 days public production): classical NRTA resolves OOIP = 1000 MSTB; Thompson-Ruddick 2022 URTeC-3718045 multiphase flowing material balance independently resolves OOIP = 1800 MSTB; the inconsistency is reconciled by fractional RTA with δ = 0.33, yielding LFP = 1.8 × 106 ft2·mdδ and OOIP = 1800 MSTB matching both methods.

The paper also formalises a seven-regime classification of flow-regime combinations in tight unconventionals (purely IA, IA → Trans, IA → Trans → BD, IA → BD, purely Trans, Trans → BD, purely BD) as pedagogical summary, and Appendix A "Cheat Sheet for Engineers" consolidates 57 equations spanning DCA, Numerical RTA, Analytical RTA, producing GOR, water cut, Darcy, compressibility, superposition, material balance, diffusivity, and fractional RTA.

Significance and Contribution

Fractional RTA as the natural generalisation. Classical RTA assumes evenly-spaced fractures and ends-of-linear-flow that match b = 2 in Arps. Real unconventional wells routinely show b ∈ (1, 2) for years after end-of-linear-flow, which classical RTA had treated as anomalous. The b = 1 / (1 - δ) identity reframes that anomaly as the signature of fracture-spacing heterogeneity, computable from a power-law fit, and integrable into the existing NRTA workflow without breaking it.

The Crow-well reconciliation is the load-bearing field example. Two independent methods (classical NRTA and Thompson-Ruddick multiphase FMB) disagree on OOIP by a factor of nearly two; fractional RTA finds the δ that makes both agree. That kind of three-method triangulation is exactly the validation that operators look for before adopting a new workflow at scale.

The seven-regime classification and the cheat-sheet appendix. Curtis's published work has rarely included a reference-tables appendix; the cheat sheet here is unusual and operationally useful, much like the 2000 monograph appendices that operators bookmark. It signals that this paper is meant as a reference, not just a results report.
