C7+ Fraction Characterisation (Book Chapter and CEC Paper)

Authors: Curtis H. Whitson, T. F. Anderson, Ingolf Søreide

Venue: (1989) Advances in Thermodynamics, Vol. 1, Taylor & Francis, pp. 35-56; and (1990) Chemical Engineering Communications, Vol. 96, pp. 259-278

Citations: not catalogued individually

C7+ Characterisation Gamma Distribution Multi-Sample Consistency Alpha-Eta Correlations TBP Distillation Phase 2

Context

A paired body of work, with the same three authors, building on Curtis's 1983 SPE-12233 framework. The Taylor & Francis book chapter (1989) is the longer-form exposition of the C7+ characterisation framework with extensions for multi-sample consistency. The Chemical Engineering Communications paper (1990) is the more technical follow-up with the empirical alpha-eta correlations and the TBP-stability analysis. Together they cover three pieces of methodology that the 1983 SPE Journal paper did not fully address: how to characterise compositionally-related samples with shared C7+ properties, how to fit the gamma distribution stably when distillation data is thin, and what the minimum data requirement is.

T. F. Anderson was at the University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign; Ingolf Søreide was Curtis's NTH colleague (later co-author of the 1992 brine-EOS paper). The Birba field (Oman) and East Painter field (Wyoming) provide the validation case studies.

Key Contributions

  • Modified β0 method for multi-sample C7+ consistency. When characterising compositionally-related samples (gas cap plus oil column, compositional grading profile, multi-well field development), all samples must share identical pseudocomponent properties Mi, Tc,i, pc,i, ωi but carry different mole fractions. Standard Gaussian quadrature breaks this because the scale parameter β varies with M7+. The fix is to fix β0 = (MN - η) / x0,N and absorb the per-sample variation in M7+ via a δ parameter that modifies the gamma PDF. Validated on Birba (oil plus gas condensate, GOR 1500 to 6200 scf/STB, simultaneous saturation-pressure match) and East Painter (8 samples, GOR 2500 to 10000 scf/STB, all saturation pressures matched within 4%).
  • Alpha-eta empirical correlations: gamma fit from three to two parameters. Fitting the three-parameter gamma distribution to TBP distillation data is unstable when distillation cuts are few. The 44-sample regression establishes that α and η are tightly correlated. For boiling point: ηT = 658 [1 - 1/(1 + 27.467/αT1.659)]; for molecular weight: ηM = 110 [1 - 1/(1 + 4.043/αM0.723)]. The limits as α → ∞ are ηT → 658°R (close to n-heptane normal boiling point) and ηM → 110 (close to toluene MW). The reduction to two parameters costs typically less than 3 AAR units across the 44 samples.
  • Minimum distillation cuts heuristic. Gamma-fit parameters are stable when N ≥ 6 distillation cuts. Below that, the three-parameter fit becomes unstable. The two-parameter alpha-eta-correlation fit is the preferred fallback for thin TBP data (N < 6). This sets a practical data-quality threshold for laboratory characterisation: a C7-C12+ analysis (6 SCN cuts) is the minimum for reliable framework input.

Significance and Contribution

The multi-sample anchor. The modified β0 method becomes the standard for any field-wide EOS development. Without it, gas-cap and oil-column compositions are characterised with different pseudocomponent properties, and the equilibrium phase prediction at the gas-oil contact is thermodynamically inconsistent. With it, all samples in a compositional grading study can share one EOS characterisation and the contacts can be predicted from the framework. The Høier-Whitson compositional grading series (1998-2004) and the Younus field-wide EOS work (2019 onwards) all depend on this multi-sample anchor.

Pedagogical companions to SPE-12233. The 1989 book chapter and 1990 CEC paper are the more accessible second-tier sources that working PVT engineers reach for after reading SPE-12233. The two-parameter alpha-eta fit is the practical workhorse when distillation data is incomplete, which is the default condition in field operations.
