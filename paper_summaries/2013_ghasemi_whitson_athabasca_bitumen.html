Context

Second in the Ghasemi-Whitson SAGD series. Whereas SPE-147072 (2011) delivered the black-oil-proxy-for-SAGD framework assuming steam only, this paper delivers the upstream fluid model needed when the SAGD process includes light-solvent co-injection (solvent-added SAGD, SA-SAGD), synthetic combustion gas (SCG: N2/CO2/CO/C1/C2), or any pure-solvent or multi-solvent augmentation. The contribution is a systematic Peng-Robinson 1979 (PR79) equation-of-state plus Lohrenz-Bray-Clark (LBC) viscosity characterisation of Athabasca bitumen built from publicly-available crude-assay data (Alberta Department of Energy 1996), validated against Svrcek-Mehrotra 1982 and Mehrotra-Svrcek 1985 bitumen-with-pure-solvent PVT data plus Svrcek-Mehrotra 1982 SCG-bitumen data. Five distinct methodological innovations span the chain from crude-assay-fit gamma distribution through single-carbon-number (SCN) characterisation to dead-oil viscosity tuning.

Key Contributions

  • 89-component EOSSCN characterisation extending to C90+. Whitson 1983 gamma molar distribution (α = 2.05, η = 98, average M = 557, RMS 1.6% against the Athabasca crude assay) drives an SCN chain out to C90+. Each SCN fraction gets specific gravity from a modified Jacoby correlation, boiling point from Soreide 1989, then critical properties from Twu 1984 with fTwu = 1 (less paraffinic, more naphthenic and aromatic - matching Athabasca's character). The 89-component model is called EOSSCN; after stepwise pseudoization to 5 pseudo-components (C7-C20, C21-C31, C32-C55, C56-C89, C90+) the model is called EOS5. Extending to C90+ is unusual for PVT characterisation but necessary because ~54 wt% of Athabasca mass distils above 524 °C and the simulator must have fractions heavier than would ever vaporise at thermal-process temperatures up to 350 °C.
  • Modified Jacoby gamma-M correlation outperforms Soreide for bitumen. Jacoby (1952) re-cast in Soreide (1989) functional form γi = A + B (Mi - C)D. Modified Jacoby parameters: A = 1.0715, B = -15.8, C = 0, D = -1, with A and B as regression variables. On Athabasca crude: modified Jacoby gives RMS 0.574% vs Soreide 1.393%. Soreide over-predicts γ at high M where the function has to flatten to match the heavy-end specific gravity.
  • Fixed BIPs plus ±10% solvent-amount adjustment as the regression strategy. One binary-interaction-parameter (BIP) value between each light solvent (CO, CO2, N2, C1, C2) and all C7+ SCN fractions, applied uniformly without temperature dependence. The regression variable is ±10% adjustment of the reported solvent amount rather than temperature-varying or component-varying BIPs. The procedure reproduces bubblepoint pressure, saturated density, and solubility to within 10% for solvent-saturated bitumen over 20-118 °C - while keeping the EOS tuning simple and physically defensible.
  • Modified Twu viscosity correlation plus linear Zc(Tb) modification brings dead-bitumen viscosity from 108 cp to measured 24,471 cp. Unmodified EOSSCN/LBC gives 1.15 × 108 cp at 100 °F - five orders of magnitude too high. The fix uses multipliers m1, m2 on SCN fractions with Tb > 1400 °R combined with a linear Zc(Tb) modification (Eq. 21 of the paper). The tuning brings dead-bitumen viscosity to measured 24,471 cp at 100 °F and matches the temperature dependence of measured bitumen viscosity over 20-200 °C.
  • L&H splitting of C90+ into lower-viscous and higher-viscous fractions for solvent-saturated bitumen viscosity. The novel contribution. EOS5 fails on solvent-saturated bitumen viscosity - LBC cannot capture the disproportionate reduction in viscosity per unit dissolved solvent. The L&H scheme splits C90+ into C90+L (lower-viscous) and C90+H (higher-viscous) with identical EOS properties (M, Tc, pc, ω, s, BIPs) but distinct critical Z-factors Zc,C90+L and Zc,C90+H. A solvent-solubility-and-temperature-correlated fraction fL sets the proportion of C90+L. The resulting six-component EOS6/LBC model predicts measured solvent-saturated bitumen viscosity across N2/CO/CO2/C1/C2 and 20-118 °C, then extrapolates to thermal-process temperatures up to 350 °C via fL-extrapolation combined with logarithmic mixing (Eq. 28).

Significance and Contribution

Completing the PVT stack underneath the SAGD proxy. Together with Ghasemi-Whitson 2011 SPE-147072 the present paper completes the PVT-and-simulation chain that makes SA-SAGD and SCG-SAGD field-scale optimisation tractable on conventional black-oil or compositional simulators. The 2011 paper showed that Rs can stand in for temperature in a black-oil proxy; this 2013 paper provides the fluid model that translates between the proxy's surrogate temperature and the real PVT of bitumen-with-solvent at thermal-process conditions.

The fluid-model-as-coverage-envelope discipline. Curtis's teaching pattern: when the downstream simulation depends critically on fluid-property accuracy (as thermal processes depend on oil viscosity), the upstream fluid-characterisation effort must cover the full range of conditions to which the simulator will push the fluid. That is why bitumen characterisation goes all the way to C90+ and validates up to 118 °C experimentally with extrapolation to 350 °C. Not because the cold-state fluid requires it - because the thermal-process operating conditions do. Systematic characterisation, not ad-hoc tuning.

Physically-defensible regression strategy. Many ultra-heavy-oil EOS papers tune temperature-dependent BIPs or component-specific volume shifts to match experimental data. The Ghasemi-Whitson approach is to hold BIPs fixed, hold C7+ volume shifts at default values, and let the reported ±10% solvent amount absorb laboratory measurement error. The result is a model that extrapolates to new solvents and new temperatures with the same internal structure.
