Gas-Oil Capillary Pressure of Chalk at Elevated Pressures

Authors: Kjell R. Christoffersen (Statoil Research Centre), Curtis H. Whitson (University of Trondheim)

Paper number: SPE-26673-MS (later in SPE Formation Evaluation)

Year: 1994

Citations: not catalogued

Capillary Pressure Chalk Ekofisk Membrane Apparatus IFT Scaling Young-Laplace Breakdown Phase 2

Context

Kjell Christoffersen's NTH PhD work. The 1992 constant-volume-diffusion experiments that prompted the Whitson-Michelsen collaboration on the diffusion code were Christoffersen's; this 1994 paper is the capillary-pressure complement of the Ekofisk PVT programme. Five md Stevns outcrop chalk is the experimental analogue for Ekofisk reservoir chalk. The fluid system is the C1-nC5 binary at 70, 105, and 130 bar (IFT 6.3, 3.2, 1.5 mN/m respectively). The headline finding overturns a piece of laboratory practice that the industry had been relying on for decades.

Key Contributions

  • Thin-membrane apparatus for fast high-pressure chalk pc. A desaturation cell using thin polymer membranes replaces the traditional 6 mm porous ceramic plate, cutting pc measurement time on 5 md chalk from months to about 14 days per complete curve. The membrane stack is a 6 μm polycarbonate track-etched membrane (0.1 μm pore diameter) sandwiched between two 105 μm cellulosic membranes. Two computer-controlled positive-displacement pumps hold the differential pressure constant to ±5 mbar across 0-1000 mbar. Saturation is tracked by mass balance on produced oil corrected for gas diffusion through the membrane. Equilibration time follows Jennings's L2/k scaling, favouring short, small-diameter plugs.
  • Young-Laplace IFT scaling breaks down below ~5 mN/m in chalk. The standard practice of scaling laboratory pc curves to reservoir IFT via pc(σ) = pcref · σ / σref assumes constant contact angle and simple proportionality. Direct high-pressure measurements on Stevns chalk show that the proportionality fails below approximately 5 mN/m. Plotted as Leverett J-function, the 6.3 and 3.2 mN/m curves nearly coincide, but the 1.5 mN/m curve sits substantially below both, especially at high wetting-phase saturation. Measured high-pressure chalk pc is roughly half what linear IFT scaling of the low-pressure centrifuge curve predicts. Two candidate physical explanations: apparent contact angle increases as IFT decreases (giving σ cosθ scaling rather than pure σ), and Cahn-transition phase redistribution in the largest pores as gas and oil approach critical properties.
  • Gravity-capillary drainage recovery in fractured chalk is underestimated by linearly-scaled laboratory pc. Engineering consequence of the IFT-scaling breakdown. Ekofisk-style gas-injection recovery is dominated by gravity-capillary drainage in which the matrix oil is forced out until gravitational head balances capillary retention. Because linear IFT scaling overestimates pc at low reservoir IFT in chalk, the equilibrium matrix saturation profile retains more oil than the true reservoir behaviour. Predicted ultimate recovery is pessimistically biased. The error is worst below 2 mN/m, which is where mature gas-condensate depletion and miscible-gas displacement designs live.

Significance and Contribution

One of the early IFT-scaling-failure papers. Through the 1980s the industry had assumed linear IFT scaling of laboratory pc curves was safe for chalk. The 1994 Christoffersen-Whitson result is the experimental demonstration that the assumption breaks at the IFT range where most operator decisions actually live. The conservative consequence of the breakdown - that linearly-scaled pc underpredicts recovery - is the kind of finding that motivates direct high-pressure measurement on the actual reservoir lithology.

Part of the Ekofisk PVT trilogy. The 1991 Hu-Whitson-Qi diffusion paper, the 1993 Riazi-Whitson IECR diffusion-coefficient correlations, and this 1994 capillary-pressure paper are the three pillars of Curtis's early-1990s framework for nitrogen-EOR PVT in fractured chalk. Together they cover the bulk-fluid transport properties, the matrix-fracture diffusion physics, and the matrix-block capillary retention. The 1991 paper's IFT-scaled pc correction is in the right direction but quantitatively insufficient below 5 mN/m; this 1994 paper is the high-pressure measurement that supersedes the linear scaling.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Weinaug-Katz parachor IFT method (calibrated with Hough-Stegemeier data)
  • Young-Laplace capillary-pressure / IFT scaling - the relationship being tested
  • Jennings t ≈ φ L2 ΔSw / (k Δpc) equilibration scaling for the apparatus design
  • Asar-Handy (1988) - gas-oil relative-permeability observations near critical, consistent with the apparent contact-angle hypothesis
  • Cahn-transition theory of phase redistribution in pores near critical conditions
  • Hu-Whitson-Qi (1991) SPE-21893 - companion nitrogen-diffusion work in the same Ekofisk programme

What It Contributed To

  • Direct high-pressure pc measurement protocols at Statoil and partner operators in the 1990s and 2000s
  • Fractured-chalk reservoir simulation studies for Ekofisk and analogues, where the linearly-scaled pc was previously used
  • The broader literature on IFT-scaling breakdown in narrow-pore-distribution rocks
  • Whitson-Brule (2000) SPE Monograph capillary-pressure section
  • Singh, Fevang & Whitson (2000) and downstream BO-vs-EOS guidelines where capillary number scaling appears in near-wellbore flow
