Numerical Investigation and Integrated Optimisation of Solvent-SAGD

Authors: Mohsen Ghasemi, Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU)

Venue: International Petroleum Technology Conference, Kuala Lumpur, 10-12 December 2014

Paper number: IPTC-18033

Citations (OpenAlex): 7

Solvent-SAGD SA-SAGD Hybrid Steam Athabasca Bitumen K-Value Classification CMG STARS Integrated Asset Management HYSYS Pipeline Phase 4

Context

Third paper of the Ghasemi-Whitson SAGD series. The 2011 SPE-147072 paper delivered the isothermal black-oil-proxy framework for pure steam; the 2013 SPE-165416 paper delivered the EOSSCN / EOS5 / EOS6 / LBC fluid model for bitumen with light or synthetic-combustion-gas (SCG) solvents; this 2014 paper combines the two into a full compositional-thermal study of hybrid solvent-augmented SAGD (SA-SAGD) via CMG STARS. The work spans single-well mechanism classification through to full integrated-asset-management (IAM) optimisation including a 100-km pipeline modelled in Hysys. Base case: 2D x-z half-symmetric Athabasca model, 30 m pay by 100 m inter-well spacing, kh = 2,000 md, kv = 1,000 md, φ = 0.33, pi = 2,200 kPa, Ti = 10 °C, cold-oil viscosity 1.33 × 106 cp, 18-component PR EOS with 5 pseudo-bitumen fractions.

Key Contributions

  • Dual-mechanism solvent classification by K-value at steam conditions. The central diagnostic. K-value at SAGD steam saturation (2,300 kPa, 219 °C) determines where the solvent condenses, which sets the recovery mechanism. Medium solvents (C5-C8) have K-values near unity - they condense at the steam-oil interface (edge oil-flow zone), dissolve into bitumen, and reduce viscosity from ~8 cp (pure SAGD) to ~0.89 cp (C7-SAGD) at the chamber edge. Heavy solvents (C12, C15) have K-values ~0.02 - they condense inside the steam chamber, remain in residual oil, and develop in-chamber micro-mobility of ~0.6 md/cp that lets otherwise-immobile oil sweep into the edge zone. Mechanism distinguishable from oil-density profiles: C7-SAGD shows sharp density drop at the chamber edge (interface dilution); C15-SAGD shows the standard SAGD density profile at the edge with composition effects concentrated inside the chamber.
  • Optimum solvent molecular weight 100-120 (between C7 and C8). The two solvent mechanisms degrade in opposite directions with molecular weight - medium-solvent edge dilution loses effectiveness as M increases above ~120 (the solvent condenses too far inside the chamber); heavy-solvent in-chamber micro-mobility loses effectiveness as M drops below ~100 (the solvent condenses too far at the edge). The intersection of the two trends gives the optimum at M = 100-120. Ultimate recovery: 88.5% for C15, 84.6% for C7, ~73% for SAGD base case.
  • Non-monotonic heavy-solvent concentration response. Increasing C15 injection concentration improves recovery up to ~20 vol% then plateaus - excess condenses near the wellbore and is back-produced rather than sweeping the chamber. Medium C6 recovery improves monotonically up to 40 vol% because edge-zone dilution scales with available solvent. Different solvent classes need different injection-concentration design rules.
  • Strong relative-permeability dependence; gas-phase endpoint matters only for heavy solvents. Straight-line oil-gas relperm (n = 1, approaching miscible behaviour) lifts ultimate C8-SAGD recovery to ~100%. The gas-phase endpoint krg(Swc) affects heavy-solvent recovery inside the chamber (because gas mobility limits the chamber-internal sweep that heavy solvents rely on) but not medium-solvent recovery at the edge (because the edge mechanism operates in the oil phase).
  • Full IAM optimisation: STARS reservoir + separator + steam generator + 100 km HYSYS pipeline. The optimisation extends the Pipe-It / Streamz IAM benchmark stack from Juell-Whitson-Hoda 2009 and Rahmawati-Whitson-Foss-Kuntadi 2010 to a thermal process chain. Optimum C6 volume fraction = 0.15 at injection pressure 2,300 kPa, shifting to 0.20 at 4,000 kPa. Optimum pipeline mixture viscosity 150 cp at bitumen/solvent flow 7,300 / 2,163 m3/d. The reservoir, surface processing, and pipeline jointly determine the economic optimum; single-element optimisation systematically underdelivers.

Significance and Contribution

Phase-behaviour classification beats trial-and-error. Curtis's teaching pattern: rather than ranking solvents by molecular weight or vapour pressure as common practice, classify them by their K-value at the process conditions. The K-value determines where in the depletion pattern the solvent does its work, which sets the mechanism, which sets the optimum design. The diagnostic is portable - it applies to other solvent-augmented thermal processes (SA-VAPEX, hybrid steam-foam) provided the K-values are evaluated at the right thermodynamic state.

The density-profile fingerprint. The diagnostic that distinguishes the two mechanisms - sharp edge-zone density drop vs internally-shifted chamber composition - parallels the density-inversion diagnostic Alavian-Whitson used in 2005 to classify CO2 recovery mechanisms in fractured reservoirs. Same family of insights: a single density-profile snapshot encodes the underlying recovery physics, no laboratory experiment needed beyond the simulation.

Coupled reservoir-process-pipeline optimisation. The 100-km HYSYS pipeline coupling matters because heavy-bitumen pipeline viscosity is the binding constraint on injection-design economics. Optimising the reservoir in isolation gives a different answer than optimising the system; the IAM framework lets the operator pick the right answer.
