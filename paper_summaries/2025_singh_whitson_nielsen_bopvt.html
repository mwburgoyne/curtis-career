Comparing Black-Oil PVT Tables with Laboratory PVT Data

Authors: Kameshwar Singh, Curtis H. Whitson, Markus Hays Nielsen (all Whitson AS)

Venue: SPE Advances in Integrated Reservoir Modelling and Field Development Conference, Abu Dhabi UAE, 2-4 June 2025

Paper number: SPE-225382-MS

Citations: New (2025)

BOPVT CCE CVD DLE PVT Groups Outlier Detection Phase 4

Context

The Whitson-Torp 1981 framework (SPE-10067) and Monograph Chapter 7 go forward: lab CVD or DLE data plus an EOS produce a black-oil PVT table for the reservoir simulator. The reverse direction has been awkward for forty years. A practitioner with a BOPVT table and a stack of laboratory PVT reports has needed to re-derive the material balance for each comparison plot. Lab data come on different reference volumes (CCE uses saturation-pressure volume, CVD uses initial-cell volume, DLE uses residual-oil volume), and the BOPVT table uses stock-tank-oil reference. A direct Bod-vs-Bo plot from raw lab numbers shows a constant offset that has nothing to do with EOS-model error, and practitioners have been re-tuning EOS models against that artefact for years.

Singh, Whitson, and Nielsen close the loop. The paper gives the closed-form material-balance equations that convert any BOPVT table into CCE-equivalent, CVD-equivalent, or DLE-equivalent quantities so that lab data and BOPVT predictions sit on one plot. It also formalises the Whitson AS "PVT groups" workflow that uses BOPVT consistency across samples to identify reservoir compartments and outlier samples. Kameshwar Singh has been Curtis's BOPVT collaborator since 2000 (SPE-63087, SPE-109596, SPE-117930, SPE-120743). Markus Hays Nielsen is Curtis's son and a Whitson AS engineer working on EOS development and PVT-uncertainty methodology (his M.Sc. at NTNU was on the effect of binary interaction parameters on phase behaviour).

Key Contributions

  • BOPVT-to-CCE conversion (Eqs 1-12). Two branches - oil sample below the bubblepoint with Rs,ref specified, gas sample below the dewpoint with rs,ref specified. From a BOPVT table (Rs, rs, Bo, Bgd) the equations give relative oil volume Vro, total relative volume Vrt, normalised oil saturation Vro* = So, phase densities, gas Z-factor, and wet-gas FVF Bg. Surface densities can be adjusted (not the EOS) to match measured-phase densities without regenerating the BOPVT table.
  • BOPVT-to-CVD stage-by-stage tracking (Eqs 13-28). Unit-reference material balance tracking surface oil N and surface gas G remaining in the PVT cell after gas removal at each depletion stage. Distinct initial conditions for gas condensate (Vro = 0 at the dewpoint, N0 = rs,0/Bgd,0) and volatile oil (Vro = 1 at the bubblepoint, N0 = 1/Bo,0). Stage-k recursion gives the produced volumes per stage, the cumulative produced surface oil and gas, the recovery factors, and the cumulative produced moles for molar-balance cross-check.
  • BOPVT-to-DLE reference-volume reconciliation (Eqs 29-30). DLE lab reports use Voer residual-oil reference while BOPVT uses Voe stock-tank reference; Bod,b > Bob and Rsd,b > Rsb by construction. Two equivalent reconciliation methods. First, oil-FVF rescale Bo = Bod(Bob/Bod,b) compares the shrinkage factor SFDLE = Bod/Bod,b against SFBOPVT = Bo/Bob as dimensionless quantities that cancel the reference-volume difference. Second, a dimensionless solution-GOR plot (Rsb - Rs)/Bob from the BOPVT table against (Rsd,b - Rsd)/Bod,b from the lab DLE eliminates the reference dependence entirely.
  • PVT-groups workflow and outlier detection. The standard Whitson AS practice for single-EOS field-wide models: generate a BOPVT table for every sample in the EOS development set, plot all samples' BOPVT-derived quantities together with their lab data, and identify samples whose BOPVT traces overlay on a common trend as one PVT group. Samples that deviate are outliers, with potential causes ranging from physical (different compartment, different fluid system) to measurement (low-quality lab PVT data) to sampling (contamination). The paper's field example identifies an outlier traced to a sample taken from a different reservoir, illustrating that the workflow catches sample-provenance errors that would have confused field-wide EOS development if blindly included.
  • Oil-viscosity depletion-type caveat. Oil viscosity from the BOPVT table is taken directly from the table at each pressure but implicitly embeds the depletion process used to generate the table (CCE vs CVD vs DLE). Lab-vs-BOPVT viscosity comparisons require matching the BOPVT depletion type to the lab experiment, otherwise the comparison shows artificial offsets that engineers may mistake for EOS-tuning error.

Significance and Contribution

Closes the BOPVT-to-lab loop. The Whitson-Torp 1981 forward direction (CVD lab data + EOS to BOPVT table) has been the standard for forty years. This paper provides the reverse direction (BOPVT table to lab-equivalent quantities) that lets engineers validate a BOPVT table against any of the three depletion experiments on one plot. The reference-volume reconciliation in particular ends the common practice of re-tuning EOS models against constant offsets that are pure reference-volume artefacts.

Operationalises field-wide PVT grouping. Younus-Whitson 2019 URTeC-551 established the single-EOS field-wide model architecture. This paper extends it from the EOS level to the BOPVT-table level: which samples in a field can share one BOPVT table, which require separate tables, which are outliers. The workflow is mechanical and reproducible.

The pedagogical use is immediate. Mark Burgoyne's bopvt-lookup library implements Eqs 1-30 directly; the Singh-Whitson-Nielsen equations are the natural follow-up exercises to the Chapter 7 BOPVT formulation in any teaching context. The paper format (closed-form equations with worked oil-sample and gas-sample figures) gives instructors a complete classroom unit.
