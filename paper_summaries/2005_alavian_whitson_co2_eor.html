Context

Haft Kel is an Iranian naturally-fractured carbonate field that has been producing since 1928 and on hydrocarbon-gas injection since 1976. Cumulative recovery under HC-gas injection sits at 15-25% of OOIP - typical for fractured carbonates where matrix-block stack height interacts with capillary entry height and viscous-gravity-capillary balances. By the early 2000s the field had become a candidate for CO2 injection. Sayyed Ahmad Alavian, then an NIOC engineer pursuing a PhD at NTNU under Whitson, ran a Sensor compositional simulation study of a single matrix block surrounded by fracture, with PhazeComp PVT and an 11-component SRK EOS, to quantify the CO2 recovery potential and the mechanisms behind it.

IPTC-10641 is the first in the long Alavian-Whitson CO2-EOR series. The collaboration continues with SPE-125362 (2009), SPE-129666 (2010), and SPE-170912 (2014), culminating in Alavian's NTNU PhD dissertation in 2011. The series threads the matrix-block analysis from Haft Kel into general fractured-reservoir CO2 EOR screening. This 2005 paper sets the framework.

Key Contributions

  • CO2-oil density inversion at ~2050 psia for Haft Kel. For the Haft Kel oil at reservoir temperature 110°F there exists a CO2-oil density inversion pressure above which CO2 gas becomes denser than the reservoir oil. Below 2050 psia CO2 < oil density (traditional gravity ordering); above 2050 psia CO2 > oil density (inverted). The crossover is a fluid property, not a reservoir property - it depends on oil composition, temperature, and CO2 purity. Lower API oils, higher temperatures, and CO2 impurities shift it. The thermodynamic Haft Kel CO2 MMP is approximately 1400 psia (slimtube) or 1350 psia (PhazeComp multi-cell), well below the inversion.
  • Two fundamentally different recovery mechanisms above and below crossover.
    • CO2 lighter than oil (P < 2050 psia). CO2 enters the matrix from the top in traditional gravity-drainage geometry. Oil produces from the bottom. Three periods: high-rate production until the gas front reaches capillary entry height; transition to lower-rate production driven by the Uleberg-Høier dynamic gravity-capillary mechanism; final slow residual production.
    • CO2 heavier than oil (P > 2050 psia). CO2 enters from the bottom. Un-equilibrated oil at the top remains near-initial saturation. An upward-moving gas front vaporises intermediates from the oil. The lightening oil behind the front, deprived of intermediates, eventually becomes heavier than the equilibrium HC-CO2 mixture and segregates downward out of the matrix bottom. Ultimate recovery at 2500 and 3000 psia reaches 92%+.
  • The density-inversion recovery minimum. Near the crossover pressure (1800-2200 psia for Haft Kel), oil recovery passes through an anomalous minimum: at 2000 psia, less than 30% of OOIP is recovered at 10,000 days vs ~100% at other pressures (Fig. 20 of source). Density differences approach zero, gravity drainage is radically slowed, and no alternative IFT-gradient mechanism has yet become dominant. Operating near the crossover is worse than operating well below it. This is a critical screening observation for any candidate fractured-reservoir CO2 EOR target.
  • IFT-gradient-driven upward Darcy flow below capillary entry height. During CO2 injection above thermodynamic MMP, near-miscibility develops at the gas-oil front. Gas-oil IFT drops sharply across a thin frontal region (e.g. from 3.8 mN/m ahead of the front to 0.06 mN/m at near-miscibility in the 2500 psia Haft Kel case). The IFT gradient creates a capillary-pressure gradient that drives oil upward, against gravity, from the low-IFT region into the higher-IFT region. Combined with the vanishing density difference at near-miscibility, this lowers the effective capillary entry height dynamically. The Uleberg-Høier (SPE-75136, 2002) mechanism is what extends recovery from 70% at 1400 days to 84% at 7400 days in the worked Haft Kel case.
  • Matrix-block height insensitivity for CO2 vs strong sensitivity for HC gas. For Haft Kel matrix blocks, HC-gas recovery scales strongly with block height: 12.9% for 6 ft blocks vs 36.7% for 14 ft blocks (Fig. 23). CO2 recovery is approximately independent of block height across 6, 8, 10, and 14 ft (Fig. 24). The dynamic IFT-gradient mechanism lowers the effective capillary entry height during CO2 displacement, so any block above the reduced threshold is fully accessible. For HC gas the entry height is a hard limit. Block-size uncertainty matters far less for CO2 than for HC gas, which is operationally significant for screening fractured reservoirs where matrix-block size distribution is poorly characterised.
  • CO2 dilution by hydrocarbon gas degrades recovery non-linearly. At 1400 psia (Haft Kel), recoveries with 50, 80, 90, and 100 mol% CO2 are 24.5%, 34.5%, 52%, and 92%. The first 10% of HC-gas dilution costs 40% of OOIP recovery. The mechanism: dilution raises the thermodynamic MMP, increases gas-oil IFT near the front, and disables the IFT-gradient mechanism. The corollary for tertiary recovery after long HC-gas cycling (Haft Kel post-1976): the fracture system is saturated with equilibrium HC gas, so achieving high CO2 mole fraction at the matrix face requires a separate fracture-system flush before CO2 injection begins. Achieving >90% CO2 at the matrix face is high-leverage engineering.
  • Rate vs ultimate-recovery decoupling. Ultimate recovery for CO2 is matrix-height insensitive, but the time to reach a given recovery still depends on matrix permeability (Fig. 27, log-log linear). CO2 project economics still require adequate matrix permeability to deliver recovery within project life - the height insensitivity argument applies to recovery, not to rate.

Significance and Contribution

The first paper in the Alavian-Whitson CO2-EOR series. IPTC-10641 establishes the matrix-block compositional-simulation framework that runs through the series: SPE-125362 (2009) on miscible CO2 displacement in fractured reservoirs, SPE-129666 (2010) on CO2-WAG, and SPE-170912 (2014) on CO2 sequestration coupled with EOR. Sayyed Ahmad Alavian's NTNU PhD dissertation (2011) consolidates the series. The 2005 paper is the methodological seed.

The density-inversion framework. The crossover-pressure construction is a screening tool. For any candidate fractured-reservoir CO2 EOR target, calculate the CO2-oil density inversion pressure with a tuned EOS. The field operating pressure relative to crossover determines which mechanism dominates and whether the anomalous recovery minimum near crossover applies. This becomes part of the screening vocabulary for fractured-reservoir CO2 projects.

The IFT-gradient mechanism quantified. Uleberg and Høier (SPE-75136, 2002) proposed the dynamic gravity-capillary mechanism for fractured CO2 injection. The 2005 Alavian-Whitson paper quantifies the mechanism for a specific reservoir and demonstrates its operational consequences (height insensitivity, dilution penalty, density-inversion minimum). The paper is the bridge from the mechanism's proposal to its application in candidate screening.

Iranian-Norwegian collaboration. The paper is also a marker of Whitson's reach. NIOC engineers are coming to NTNU for PhD work on Iranian reservoirs; the published output goes back into Iranian field-development planning. Alavian's pipeline (NIOC engineer, NTNU PhD candidate, eventual NTNU PhD 2011) is one of several international student threads in Whitson's Phase 3 supervisory record.
