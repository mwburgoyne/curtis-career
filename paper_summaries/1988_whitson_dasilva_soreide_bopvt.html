Simplified Compositional Formulation for Modified Black-Oil Simulators

Authors: Curtis H. Whitson (NTH), Flavio da Silva (Petrofina S.A.), Ingolf Søreide (NTH)

Venue: 63rd SPE Annual Technical Conference and Exhibition, Houston, 2-5 October 1988

Paper number: SPE-18315

DOI: 10.2118/18315-MS

Citations: 12, ; influence runs through SIMBEST II and the wider modified-black-oil practice

Generalised Black-Oil Modified Black-Oil Compositional Variation Non-Equilibrium Injection SIMBEST Phase 2

Context

By the late 1980s the Norwegian operators were facing two simulation problems that fell between the standard tools. North Sea volatile-oil reservoirs - especially the near-critical fields - showed strong areal and depth-varying surface-oil composition. Traditional black-oil could not represent that. The other problem was nitrogen injection (and other non-equilibrium gas injection) for pressure maintenance and EOR. Fully compositional EOS simulation was the right answer in principle but the run times were prohibitive for full-field studies.

Curtis was thirty-two and had founded Whitson AS earlier in 1988. Flavio da Silva was at Petrofina (later Total) and was driving the Ekofisk nitrogen-injection analysis. Ingolf Søreide was Curtis's NTH PhD student finishing the brine-EOS work that would land in Fluid Phase Equilibria in 1992.

Key Contributions

  • The GBO four-component formulation. Extend the two-component modified black-oil (MBO) model to four surface components:
    1. Non-volatile surface oil (in reservoir liquid only)
    2. Volatile surface oil (partitions between liquid and vapour, the MBO surface oil)
    3. Hydrocarbon surface gas (partitions between phases, the MBO surface gas)
    4. Injection gas (non-equilibrium: N2, CO2, or a rich HC injection gas)
    GBO reduces to traditional BO when only components 1 and 3 are present, and to MBO when 2 and 3 are present without 1.
  • The state variable r for compositional variation. A single scalar r = (γoL - γ2) / (γ1 - γ2) measures the relative fraction of non-volatile to volatile surface oil in each grid cell's reservoir liquid. PVT properties are blended from two pre-computed binary tables: Rs = r Rs1 + (1-r) Rs2, and analogously for BL, μL, and rs. Compositional variation becomes a one-dimensional problem the simulator indexes by r.
  • Tau and sigma for injection-gas partitioning. With component 4 present, two more scalars track injection gas: σ = y4/(y3+y4) in the reservoir vapour and τ = x4/(x3+x4) in the liquid. A two-parameter relation τ = b σn + (1-b) σm closes the system. For Ekofisk nitrogen injection b, n, m are around 1.5, 1.1, 1.0.
  • Material-balance procedure for the binary properties (Appendix B). Binary 1-3 properties follow from standard Lasater-Glaso-Beggs-Robinson correlations (non-volatile oil, well-behaved). Binary 2-3 properties are extracted from one DLE experiment by subtraction: Rs2 = (Rs - r Rs1) / (1-r), and so on. Material balance on moles n1, n2, n3 through the depletion stages tracks the correct r(p). A practical aside: back-calculation of μL2 from blending can give negative values, so use a correlation in γ2, Rs2, T even though it is algebraically inconsistent.
  • Regression on binary 1-4 / 2-4 properties (Appendix C). With injection gas, the blending involves four binaries. Binary 1-4 and 2-4 properties are not available from correlations because they involve injection gas. They are obtained by matching one or more multi-contact injection experiments (or EOS-generated equivalent data) via non-linear regression on the binary properties and the τ(σ) parameters b, n, m.
  • The honest limitations. Two cases GBO as published cannot handle. Gas-condensate compositional variation: component 1 is non-volatile by definition, so it cannot dissolve in the initial reservoir gas, and the surface-oil composition is implicitly uniform. Cycling below the dewpoint: the GBO default predicts optimistic revaporisation of condensed liquid; setting rs24 = 0 suppresses it but overcorrects when injection gas contains meaningful component 3. Curtis flags both as future-work targets in conclusion 4.

Significance and Contribution

Filling the gap between MBO and EOS simulation. Through the late 1980s and 1990s GBO occupied the practical middle ground between the cheap-but-inadequate MBO and the rigorous-but-expensive EOS. The Ekofisk nitrogen-injection studies and similar Norwegian operator work in the period used GBO as the production tool. The formulation is deployed in SIMBEST II.

The structural insight. Separating compositional variation (r) from injection-gas partitioning (τ, σ) is what makes GBO tractable. Without it, the same problem requires a full compositional simulator. Three scalars carry most of the relevant physics; the rest of the computational cost is shifted to one-time PVT-table generation.

The honesty about limits. Conclusion 4 of the paper explicitly lists what GBO cannot do. This is the same disciplined framing that runs through Curtis's work: a method is sold for the cases where it works, and the cases where it fails are documented up-front rather than discovered by users.

Bridge to the Singh-Whitson BOPVT series. The black-oil-PVT generation framework matured into the Singh-Whitson series from 2000 onwards (SPE-63087, SPE-109596, SPE-117930), which formalises black-oil-property generation from a tuned EOS. GBO is the conceptual precursor that demonstrated the modified-black-oil framework could carry serious compositional physics.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Kniazeff & Naville (1965) - the original volatile-oil formulation that motivates the MBO and GBO extensions
  • Spivak & Dixon (1974) - the modified-black-oil formulation that GBO generalises
  • Whitson (1983) SPE-12233 and (1984) SPE-12634 - the C7+ and EOS-tuning machinery feeding GBO's binary-property generation
  • Coats (1980) - compositional reservoir simulation that GBO offers a cheaper approximation to
  • Lasater (1958), Glasø (1980), Beggs & Robinson (1975), Standing (1947) - the empirical PVT correlations supplying binary 1-3 properties for non-volatile oil + HC gas

What It Contributed To

  • Ekofisk nitrogen-injection studies through the 1990s and beyond
  • The Singh-Whitson BOPVT series (2000-2010) - SPE-63087, SPE-109596, SPE-117930 - which formalises black-oil-property generation from a tuned EOS
  • Whitson-Brule (2000) SPE Phase Behavior monograph, Chapter 7, on modified-black-oil PVT
  • Subsequent whitson black-oil-property workflows in 2020s field studies
  • Demonstrated to the simulator vendors that intermediate fluid models had a place in production workflows
