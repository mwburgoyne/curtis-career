Target-Rate Tracking for Shale-Gas Multi-Well Pads by Scheduled Shut-Ins

Authors: Brage Rugstad Knudsen, Bjarne Foss, Curtis H. Whitson, A. R. Conn (NTNU / IBM T. J. Watson)

Venue: 8th IFAC Symposium on Advanced Control of Chemical Processes (ADCHEM), Singapore, 10-13 July 2012, pp. 107-113

Citations: not catalogued

Shale Gas Multi-Well Pad MILP Scheduling Scheduled Shut-Ins Liquid Loading Turner Rate Radial Composite Proxy Phase 4

Context

Brage Knudsen's PhD work at NTNU under Bjarne Foss (Engineering Cybernetics); Curtis provided the petroleum-engineering domain foundation, and A. R. Conn (IBM T. J. Watson Research) provided optimisation-theory input. The paper proposes a scheduling scheme for shale-gas multi-well pads that performs two jobs simultaneously: avoid liquid loading via Turner-rate monitoring, and track a midstream-operator target rate by strategically shutting in individual wells. The scheduling problem is cast as a mixed-integer linear program (MILP) with binary choke-opening variables, using an exact linear reformulation of a nonlinear min-operator well model. The reservoir side is a radial composite proxy model with three concentric regions (high-permeability fracture, intermediate transition, low-permeability matrix) tuned against a high-fidelity SENSOR multi-fracture simulation. The work extends Rahmawati-Whitson-Foss 2009 (single-well constant cyclic shut-ins) and Whitson-Rahmawati-Juell 2012 SPE-153073 (cyclic shut-in control) into a multi-well variable-duration scheme embedded in a plantwide-control framework.

Key Contributions

  • Three-region radial composite proxy for a multi-fractured horizontal shale-gas well. Concentric regions: inner high-permeability fractured volume (example fracture permeability 100 mD), intermediate transition region with averaged properties, outer low-permeability shale matrix (example matrix permeability 3.5 × 10-4 mD). The intermediate transition regularises the ~106 permeability contrast that would otherwise cause numerical problems in pure dual-permeability models, while also representing the physical reality that the fracture-matrix transition is unlikely to be as abrupt as the dual-permeability idealisation. Gas flow described by the radial pseudopressure PDE with implicit backward-Euler time-stepping over a logarithmic-radial grid of 12 blocks. Tuned against a SENSOR multi-fracture reference (10 perpendicular hydraulic fractures, ~2,500 cells) over a 70-day horizon (days 295-365 after startup) with two predefined shut-ins (7-day and 10-day). Weighted sum-of-squares objective with heavy weights on the post-shut-in rate-decline regions because the proxy is destined for shut-in scheduling.
  • Mixed-integer-linear-program scheduling of well shut-ins for joint liquid-loading avoidance and target-rate tracking. Control variables are binary choke openings αk ∈ {0, 1} for each well at each time step. Objective: maximise discounted gas revenue minus surplus and shortfall penalties relative to the midstream target rate. Constraints: each well operates above the Turner liquid-loading rate when producing (disjunctive constraint qgc(pw) αk ≤ qk), minimum shut-in duration τ ≥ 5 days (Floudas 1995 switching-count auxiliary variables), an exact linear reformulation of the well-model min-operator and the bilinear αk · m1,k product via big-M technique, and the reservoir radial composite proxy embedded as explicit constraints. Eight-well 28-day numerical example: 4,234 continuous variables + 456 binary variables + 5,701 constraints. The optimised shut-in schedule gives 4.1% higher cumulative gas than a naive heuristic of constant one-week shut-ins, with a much less erratic pad-total rate.
  • Turner-rate disjunctive constraint without water saturation in the reservoir model. The Turner 1969 critical-gas-velocity rate qgc(pw) for liquid lift is evaluated at wellhead conditions, so the reservoir-side proxy can remain single-phase dry gas without carrying explicit water saturation state. The disjunctive constraint qgc(pw) αk ≤ qk collapses trivially when the well is shut in (αk = 0, so 0 ≤ qk = 0) and enforces Turner's lower bound when the well is producing (qgc(pw) ≤ qk). The Floudas 1995 technique embeds the disjunctive boundary-condition control law directly in the MILP without adding binary variables. The reservoir model can be single-phase dry gas - the liquid-loading physics is captured as a wellhead boundary condition.

Significance and Contribution

Shut-ins as a primary control handle. Outside drilling new wells, the few available production handles on a shale-gas pad are the production choke and well shut-ins. Shut-ins are already needed for routine maintenance, liquid-loaded-well cleanup, and during nearby-well fracturing jobs. The insight is that shut-ins can be scheduled, not just reactive, so that the same control handle simultaneously avoids liquid loading and tracks the target rate.

The plantwide-control framing. The MILP framework anchors shale-gas production optimisation in standard chemical-process plantwide-control language (Floudas-1995-style disjunctive reformulation, big-M, switching-count variables). The Whitson contribution is the petroleum-engineering side - the radial composite proxy, the Turner-rate trigger formulation, the connection to the Whitson-Rahmawati-Juell 2012 cyclic-shut-in-control concept. The combination opens shale-gas operations to optimisation-theory tools developed in chemical engineering.

The proxy-for-high-fidelity pattern. Same pattern as the Ghasemi-Whitson 2011 SAGD black-oil proxy: single-case high-fidelity calibration (here SENSOR multi-fracture; there STARS thermal), then many-case proxy sweep (here MILP scheduling; there SAGD design optimisation). The compactness and linearity-in-pseudopressure of this proxy is what makes full-space MILP scheduling tractable; the portability of the SAGD n is what makes that proxy optimisation-ready. Different problems, same proxy-design principle.
