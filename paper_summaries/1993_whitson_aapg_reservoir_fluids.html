Context

A four-page entry in the AAPG Development Geology Reference Manual, written for a geologist audience rather than a reservoir-engineer audience. Most of the content (fluid-property definitions, the five-category reservoir-fluid classification, the PVT experiment overview, basic correlations for FVF and Z-factor, reservoir-water properties) is later subsumed by the 2000 Whitson-Brule Monograph and is already documented elsewhere in the corpus.

What is unique to this chapter is one Whitson operational heuristic that the Monograph treats more implicitly: the rule of thumb for when gas cycling becomes economically justified by initial liquid content.

Key Contribution

  • Gas cycling economic-justification thresholds by liquid content. Cycling produced gas (after condensate stripping) back into a gas-condensate reservoir is economically justified when the initial solution oil-gas ratio is high enough that revaporisation of retrograde condensate offsets the cycling capex and opex. Whitson's screening thresholds:
    • Onshore: OGR > ~50-100 STB/MMSCF at initial reservoir conditions (roughly 3-6 mol% C5+)
    • Offshore: OGR > ~100 STB/MMSCF (the higher floor reflects offshore facility capex and gas-handling infrastructure cost)
    Below these thresholds, pressure depletion with dry-gas sales and acceptance of retrograde condensate loss is typically the better economic outcome. Below the threshold, alternatives include nitrogen injection (cheaper than lean-gas cycling when N2 is locally available), blowdown after partial cycling, or simple depletion.

Significance and Contribution

A screening rule that travels. The onshore-vs-offshore gap is a practical Whitson framing: offshore facility costs roughly double the cycling break-even OGR, which is why many offshore gas-condensate fields are produced by depletion even when onshore analogues of similar richness are cycled. The numbers are 1993-vintage and are often quoted from this AAPG chapter; engineers update them to local cost bases.

Writing for geologists. The chapter is also a Curtis signature - the willingness to write a pedagogical entry for an adjacent discipline. The 2000 Monograph covers gas cycling at full technical weight; the AAPG chapter is the version a development geologist actually reads when sizing a cycling project's surface envelope.
