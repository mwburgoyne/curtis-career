An Engineering Approach to Measuring and Modelling Gas Condensate Relative Permeabilities

Authors: Arne Sævareid, Curtis H. Whitson, Øivind Fevang

Venue: Society of Core Analysts International Symposium, paper SCA-9930, 1999, 13 pp.

Paper number: SCA-40899

Citations: Not indexed in OpenAlex; cited through the 2003 TIPM consolidation (29 OpenAlex)

Context

The 1996 Fevang-Whitson three-region model and the 1997 IBC generalisation rest on one experimental claim: that krg = f(krg/kro) is the fundamental relationship for Region 1 deliverability, and that it can be measured directly without resolving in-situ saturation. The 1999 SCA paper is the laboratory programme that delivers on the claim. Arne Sævareid built the ResLab closed-loop apparatus at NTH; Whitson and Fevang supplied the theoretical framework that lets the apparatus skip saturation measurement entirely. The paper reports steady-state measurements on Berea and North Sea sandstone, tests three categories of hysteresis under realistic flow cycling, and proposes a rock-property-scaled calibration for the capillary-number transition function.

The paper is part of the Gas Condensate Well Performance project (sponsors BP, Chevron, INA, Mobil, Norsk Hydro, NFR, NPD, Occidental, Saga) that ran 1996-1999 and produced the 1996 SPE-30714 three-region paper, the 1997 IBC generalisation, this 1999 measurement paper, and the 2003 TIPM consolidation.

Key Contributions

  • Closed-loop apparatus for long-duration steady-state measurement. A closed-loop circuit recycles produced condensate gas through a high-pressure booster pump and a re-equilibration cylinder back to the injection side, rather than consuming fresh synthetic gas-condensate fluid for every pore volume. Specifications: 700 bar / 180°C maximum; composite cores up to 50 cm length, 1.5 inch diameter; independent axial and radial confining load up to 1000 bar. The design lets lean-condensate measurements run for hundreds of pore volumes (which they need to, at krg/kro > 50) without depleting expensive synthetic fluid.
  • Measurement programme bypasses saturation. For each target krg/kro value, select a two-phase mixture whose CCE oil-relative-volume Vro at core-face pressure gives the desired ratio via the Evinger-Muskat relation krg/kro = (1/Vro - 1)(μg/μo). Flow at steady state. Record Δp, qg, qo, viscosities. Compute krg and kro from Darcy's law. The krg/kro range encountered over a real well's life is about one order of magnitude (rich: 1 to 10; lean: 1 to 50). The krg range is 0.05 to 0.3 for most systems. Five well-chosen mixtures span the relevant range.
  • No significant hysteresis under realistic flow cycling. Three hysteresis categories tested:
    • Velocity hysteresis. Varying the flowing gas rate (9 to 59 ml/min, three times base) at fixed pressure and fixed krg/kro changes krg by at most 5-10% (rich) or 5-20% (lean, low-perm). No systematic trend with rate.
    • Shut-in / drawdown hysteresis. A 10-12 hour shut-in at 375 bar (returning the core to single-phase hydrocarbon), then reopening at 100 bar flowing pressure, recovers the base krg within 2-3 pore volumes. Final steady-state krg = f(krg/kro) is independent of the core's initial saturation state.
    • Variable-pressure (multirate well test) hysteresis. Ramping core pressure from 100 bar up to 275 bar then back at constant 30 ml/min recovers the base krg within ten pore volumes.
    The implication for simulation: a single krg = f(krg/kro) relation can be used for reservoir simulation without a hysteresis model. Decades of shut-ins, rate changes, and pressure cycles do not substantively degrade the kr response in sandstone reservoirs. This is an operationally valuable negative result.
  • The α-parameter scales with rock as α = 104/√(kφ). The Whitson-Fevang capillary-number transition function f = 1 - [(α Nc)n + 1]-1 has a rock-dependent scaling parameter that correlates with permeability (md) and porosity (fraction) by Leverett-J-function reasoning. Berea fit (Fig. 7 of source): α = 2000 consistent with k ≈ 135 md, φ ≈ 0.19, exponent n ≈ -0.64, immiscible end-point krgI = 0.085, miscible end-point krgM = 0.353. The fit collapses two fluids (synthetic at krg/kro = 1.6, reservoir at krg/kro = 2.8) with very different IFTs (0.8-1.6 vs 6.3 mN/m) onto a single curve. Rock heterogeneity enters through kφ, not through a free-fit α.
  • Saturation measurement is optional, not central. If conversion back to a conventional kr(S) format is wanted (for legacy reservoir simulators that lack the Whitson-Fevang procedure), the closed-loop apparatus can measure saturation after the flow test by displacing the produced condensate, re-equilibrating with reservoir gas at core conditions, and measuring the liquid volume. Fitting an immiscible Corey or Chierici correlation against the measured krg(krg/kro) data gives the saturation form. The point: saturation is an output, not a controlled variable.
  • Range targeting is the methodological lever. Conventional SCAL practice runs full kr(S) envelopes from connate water to residual oil. The Whitson-Fevang programme runs five points across the krg/kro range expected during the well's economic life. This saves weeks of laboratory time for the engineering outcome that actually matters, which is Region 1 deliverability.

Significance and Contribution

The experimental backbone of the Fevang-Whitson framework. SPE-30714 (1996) proposed the three-region model; the 1997 IBC paper generalised it; SCA-40899 measured the relationships the framework relies on. The closed-loop apparatus is the platform for every subsequent gas-condensate kr measurement that targets krg = f(krg/kro) directly. The no-hysteresis finding for sandstone reservoirs is the licence that lets simulation teams skip the hysteresis modelling layer with confidence.

The α = 104/√(kφ) scaling. Capillary-number dependence of gas-condensate kr is a rock property (via α) and a fluid property (via σ inside Nc). The Leverett-J-style scaling separates the two so that one calibration measurement on a representative rock predicts the response across a heterogeneous reservoir. The 2003 TIPM paper updates this to α = 2×104/√(kφ) with the combined Heriot-Watt + Delft data, but the structural insight (α scales with 1/√(kφ)) is from this paper.

Sævareid's role. Arne Sævareid built the apparatus and ran the measurement campaign. Whitson and Fevang's contribution is the theoretical framework that turns the apparatus into a Region 1 deliverability lab. The collaboration is the experimentalist-theorist pairing that runs through the GCB project: ResLab supplies the data, the NTH/Whitson AS group supplies the framework.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Fevang & Whitson (1996) SPE-30714 — the three-region model and the krg = f(krg/kro) framework
  • Whitson & Fevang (1997) IBC — the generalised pseudopressure with the original Whitson-Fevang capillary-number form (α free, n ≈ -0.75)
  • Evinger & Muskat (1942), Muskat (1949) — the steady-state two-phase identity krg/kro = (1/Vro - 1)(μg/μo) that makes saturation-free measurement possible
  • Henderson et al. (1995, 1996, 1998) Heriot-Watt steady-state gas-condensate kr measurements — complementary dataset
  • Ham & Eilerts (1967) — the early high-pressure gas-condensate kr work the closed-loop apparatus revisits with modern instrumentation
  • Leverett J-function (1941) — the √(k/φ) scaling of capillary forces

What It Contributed To

  • Whitson, Fevang & Sævareid (2003) TIPM — the consolidated journal treatment with updated α = 2×104/√(kφ) and n = 0.7 fit to combined Heriot-Watt + Delft data, and the monovariance proof attributed to Panfilov
  • Whitson & Brule (2000) SPE Monograph "Phase Behavior", Chapter 7 — the textbook presentation of the gas-condensate kr laboratory programme
  • The ResLab closed-loop apparatus design is the reference platform for subsequent industry gas-condensate kr measurement
  • The no-significant-hysteresis finding shaped commercial simulator decisions to forgo hysteresis branches for gas-condensate kr in sandstone reservoirs
  • The α(kφ) Leverett scaling underpins the 2003 TIPM endpoint-scaling procedure for heterogeneous strata
  • Whitson AS field studies on Sleipner, Eldfisk, and other North Sea gas-condensate fields used the saturation-free measurement programme to size and design lab campaigns
