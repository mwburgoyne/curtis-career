Authors: Aleksander Oma Juell, Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU)

Venue: SPE Annual Technical Conference and Exhibition, New Orleans, 30 September - 2 October 2013

Paper number: SPE-166380

DOI: 10.2118/166380-MS

Citations (OpenAlex (DOI)): 20

Context

The fourth and final paper of Aleksander Juell's NTNU PhD with Curtis. Integrates single-well finite-difference (FD) modelling with economic optimisation for liquid-rich shale (LRS) wells. Applications cited for Eagle Ford, Bakken, Avalon, and Montney plays (no field data publishable). The paper threads six distinct methodological contributions through one workflow: half-fracture symmetry-element FD model with system-specific grid-refinement guidelines, explicit modelling of treatment-fluid injection and flowback, an operational recommendation to install bottomhole pressure gauges for the first 2-4 weeks of LRS production, identification of three key history-match parameters with productivity-term xf√k non-uniqueness, revenue-optimal drawdown control with a two-period strategy for highly-undersaturated gas-condensate (GC), and net-present-value (NPV) optimisation over fracture count plus horizontal length plus fracture half-length. Built on the SENSOR black-oil simulator (Coats Engineering) plus Pipe-It integration stack.

Key Contributions

  • Half-fracture symmetry-element FD model with system-specific grid refinement. Only a half-fracture segment of the well is gridded (one fracture, one side of wellbore); rates scale up to full-well during post-processing. Compared with a fully-gridded full-well model, the half-fracture model's producing OGR matches within plotting scatter throughout an 18,000-day forecast. Geometric grid spacing along the wellbore ri = ri-1 (Lh/(Nf wf))1/N. Refinement guidelines tier by fluid system: oil LRS converges at 80 × 20 cells per half-fracture (1,600 total); near-saturated GC LRS tolerates much coarser grids because producing OGR is dominated by solution OGR at flowing bottomhole pressure (FBHP); highly undersaturated GC LRS (e.g., pi = 7,000 psia, psat = 4,250 psia) requires 200 × 40 cells (8,000 total) because the steeper pressure gradient near fractures demands finer resolution. CPU speedup of roughly 2Nf (~40x at 20 fractures).
  • Treatment-fluid injection and flowback via stress-dependent transmissibility and water capillary pressure. Combines the Jurus-Whitson-Golan 2013 SPE-166439 stress-dependent permeability multiplier k/k0 = 10m(p-pi) with water capillary pressure curves Pcwo = b1 + b2(1 - (Sw-Swc)/(1-Swc))b3. The stress multiplier admits the physical injection volume (~80,000 STB per fracture stage typical) within the treatment window. The capillary-pressure form retains treatment water in the matrix: without Pc, 80% of injected water flows back within a year; with b3 = 3, only ~40% does. An optional post-injection shut-in period drives water deeper into the matrix via capillary imbibition.
  • Install bottomhole pressure gauges for the first 2-4 weeks of LRS production. During the early weeks of LRS production, multi-phase flow in the production string (treatment-fluid flowback plus hydrocarbons) causes pressure-drop uncertainty of up to 3,000 psi (~40%) across standard correlations (Fetkovich backpressure, Duns-Ros, Orkiszewski, Beggs-Brill, Hagedorn-Brown, Gray, Petroleum Experts) when water-gas ratio is non-zero. Once water cut drops, correlations converge to within ~10%. A $10-30k bottomhole memory gauge for the first 2-4 weeks is a high-ROI investment because FBHP is the single most important calibration input for an LRS FD model.
  • Three key history-match parameters with productivity-term non-uniqueness. Matrix permeability k, fracture half-length xf, and initial in-situ fluid composition (solution GOR or OGR) account for almost all long-term performance variability. The product xf√k is the well's effective productivity term, and over the long transient any model with a given productivity term has similar long-term performance - meaning xf and k cannot be independently determined from rate data alone. The relative-permeability exponent for oil nog is confounded with initial fluid composition in OGR fitting (Fig. 25). Resolve by establishing initial fluid composition from PVT sampling per Whitson-Sunjerga 2012 and reporting the economic-forecast sensitivity over the admissible xf-k envelope.
  • Optimal drawdown for highly-undersaturated GC: pwf ≈ psat in period 1, then ramp down. The counter-intuitive finding. For highly-undersaturated gas-condensate LRS (e.g., pi = 9,000 psia, psat = 4,250 psia), maximum-drawdown is the wrong policy. The revenue-vs-pwf curve peaks at pwf ≈ psat because pulling below psat causes near-wellbore condensate blockage that permanently sacrifices mobility. Optimal two-period strategy: 100-170 days at pwf = 4,250 psia, then gradual reduction to the operationally-minimum. Going too short on period 1 (e.g., 50 days) loses revenue from incomplete condensate-bank stabilisation; going too long wastes late revenue. Reverse-sequence strategies (low pwf first, then high) permanently lose revenue - once the bank forms, reducing drawdown cannot recover lost mobility.
  • NPV optimisation: ~45 fractures optimal for oil LRS, ~30 for GC LRS; fracture area dominates over distribution. Cost model Cw = a0 + a1 Lh + a2 Nf (xf/xf0)a3 with a0 = $9 × 105 (vertical drilling), a1 = $350/ft horizontal, a2 = $2 × 105/stage at reference xf0 = 150 ft, base-case total cost ~$7 × 106. NPV discounted at 10%, oil $100/STB, gas $3/Mscf. Optimum Nf: ~45 for oil (NPV peak ~$1.2 × 107), ~30 for GC (NPV peak ~$2.5 × 106). Crucially: keeping total fracture area Nf × xf constant while varying Nf and xf yields essentially flat NPV - the number of perforation clusters per stage is not significant for a given total area, decoupling "how much to frac" (economics) from "how to distribute" (completion engineering).

Significance and Contribution

Time-dependent control policy. The drawdown-optimum result inverts the conventional maximise-drawdown wisdom for highly-undersaturated GC. There is no single best pwf - only a sequence of pwf values that together maximise cumulative revenue. This is the time-dependent-control-policy extension of single-point optimisation, and it shows up because the near-wellbore condensate blockage is hysteretic: mobility lost to blockage never recovers.

Honest non-uniqueness. Recognising structural non-uniqueness early saves the analyst from chasing a false optimum:

The three most important history matching parameters are matrix permeability (k), fracture half-length (xf), and initial fluid in-place (solution OGR or composition). In reality the long transient production performance is controlled by the wells "productivity term" xf√k.

Rather than over-fitting xf vs k through implausible parameter combinations, accept the bundle and report the economic-forecast sensitivity. Make the uncertainty envelope visible.

The fracture-area-dominance result. When an optimisation dimension turns out to be insensitive (fracture-area distribution along the wellbore), report that fact prominently. The value is in telling the completion engineer "pick the clusters-per-stage pragmatically, the economics don't care". Time saved on fake optimisation is value added.
