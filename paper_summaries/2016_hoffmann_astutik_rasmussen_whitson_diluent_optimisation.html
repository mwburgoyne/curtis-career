Diluent Injection Optimisation for a Heavy Oil Field

Authors: A. Hoffmann, Wahyu Astutik, F. Rasmussen (Statoil), Curtis H. Whitson (NTNU)

Venue: SPE Heavy Oil Conference and Exhibition, Kuwait City, 6-8 December 2016

Paper number: SPE-184119

Citations: not catalogued

Heavy Oil Diluent Injection ESP MILP SOS2 Piecewise-Linear Brinkman Emulsion Inversion Point RTPO Pipe-It Phase 4

Context

Hoffmann's NTNU MSc thesis (2014, "Application of Piecewise Linear Models to Short-Term Oil Production Optimization") under Curtis's supervision is the theoretical basis. The paper presents an integrated modelling solution for an offshore heavy-oil field with two reservoirs (L: 14 °API, 70 cp; H: 12 °API, 500 cp), 60 electric-submersible-pump (ESP) lifted wells, and diluent injection at either each well's ESP intake or surface pre-second-stage separator. Objective: maximise cumulative reservoir oil production while minimising diluent consumption and honouring field, process, and well constraints. The contribution extends the Whitson Pipe-It integrated-asset-management (IAM) heritage from processes with binary or discrete decision variables to problems dominated by continuous non-linearities. First Whitson MILP-optimisation paper to apply Special-Ordered-Set-of-type-2 (SOS2) piecewise linearisation rather than binary shut-in scheduling. Also doubles as a Real-Time Production Optimisation (RTPO) tool with use cases for new test data, compressor or pump failure, facility down, workover, and new-well startup.

Key Contributions

  • MILP formulation with SOS2 piecewise-linear relaxation of two coupled non-linearities. The two non-linearities: per-well performance curves qores = f(DC, pwh) where DC = diluent cut, and the ASTM D7152-11 volumetric viscosity mixing rule for the two crude-quality specifications (technical viscosity less than 10 cp at 105 °C; market viscosity less than 400 cSt at 40 °C). SOS2 relaxation: ƒ˜(x) = ∑ λi · f(xi), x = ∑ λi xi, ∑ λi = 1, with binary variables enforcing the adjacency-of-non-zeros condition. The resulting MILP is solvable by simplex + branch-and-bound in 0.1-5 seconds per time step with global-optimum guarantee. Table regeneration (PROSPER + Pipe-It rebuild of the 60-well performance tables) is the CPU bottleneck at 1-3 minutes per regeneration.
  • Two-PVT-per-well model with DC-dependent upper leg. Classical inflow-performance-relation / vertical-lift-performance (IPR / VLP) qores = f(pR, pwh, WC, GOR, PI) extended to qores = f(DC, pwh) via Brinkman 1952 emulsion viscosity in oil-continuous flow and an experimentally-measured DC-shift of the emulsion inversion point. Commercial well-performance simulators do not accept multiple fluid models per well, so the wellbore is split at the ESP intake into a lower leg using reservoir-oil PVT and an upper leg using diluted-oil PVT. The pressure-drop calculation iterates between the two legs in Pipe-It using Brent 1970 root-finding to match the intake pressure.
  • Optimal-allocation case-study finding: 9x less diluent for 1.6 points more recovery uplift. Optimal DC allocation across 60 wells produces 50.8 MMsm3 cumulative reservoir oil (+6.3% vs base case) with only 0.53 MMsm3 diluent demand, versus 50.0 MMsm3 (+4.6%) and 4.57 MMsm3 diluent for naive constant allocation (5% DC for wells in reservoir L, 10% DC for reservoir H). The optimiser concentrates injection 2054-2060 when platform liquid and oil-handling capacity is the dominant constraint, and stops injecting after 2060 because field water cut has moved past all wells' emulsion-inversion points (above which more diluent actually increases mixture viscosity). Naive constant allocation does the opposite - injects when not needed and underinvests when it matters.
  • Diluent-type sensitivity decoupled from market price. Three diluent API grades (45, 49, 51 °API) give identical cumulative reservoir-oil recovery (50.80 / 50.84 / 50.80 MMsm3) but substantially different diluent demand (0.59 / 0.53 / 0.40 MMsm3) and flash loss (4-9 vol% burned as fuel gas, higher for lighter diluent). The recovery insensitivity to grade is a useful negative result: diluent selection must co-optimise market price, flash loss, and viscosity efficacy rather than minimising demand alone.

Significance and Contribution

The right convexification for continuous-decision IAM. Curtis's teaching pattern: when a non-linear optimisation problem resists global solution, find the relaxation that makes it tractable. SOS2 piecewise linearisation converts a multi-well multi-period non-linear allocation problem (normally requiring heuristic methods with no optimality guarantee) to an MILP for which simplex plus branch-and-bound converge to the global optimum. Runtime 0.1-5 seconds per time step is RTPO-compatible. The previous Whitson IAM benchmarks (Juell-Whitson-Hoda 2009 gas cycling, Rahmawati-Whitson-Foss-Kuntadi 2010 multi-field) used Nelder-Mead-class heuristic optimisers because the decision variables were continuous; SOS2 is the upgrade that brings optimality guarantees.

Inversion-point physics turns out to be load-bearing. The water-cut and DC dependence of the emulsion inversion point is the operational subtlety that the optimiser exploits. Naive intuition says "more diluent → lower viscosity → more oil". The truth is non-monotonic: above the inversion point, more diluent flips the emulsion phase and increases viscosity. The optimiser knows this; constant-allocation policies do not. The 9x diluent saving comes from respecting the inversion-point physics.

RTPO as natural by-product. Because the MILP solves in seconds, the same model serves as a real-time decision-support tool. Well-test measurements update well-performance tables via Hoda-Whitson 2013 SPE-164334 well-test rate-to-compositional-wellstream conversion, enabling compositional-basis reservoir-oil / diluent blending in Pipe-It. Kuntadi-Whitson-Hoda 2012 SPE-159400 black-oil delumping supplies reservoir-oil composition from the black-oil reservoir simulator. The whole Pipe-It / Streamz stack assembles into a real-time optimisation system.
