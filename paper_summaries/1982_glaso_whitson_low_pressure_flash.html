The Accuracy of PVT Parameters Calculated From Computer Flash Separation at Pressures Less Than 1,000 psia

Authors: Øystein S. Glasø, Curtis H. Whitson

Venue: Journal of Petroleum Technology, JPT Forum, August 1982, pp. 1811-1813

Citations: not catalogued

Low-Pressure Flash K-Value Heuristic Katz-Hachmuth Standing 1979 Heptanes-Plus Phase 1

Context

By 1982 Curtis is twenty-six, finishing his PhD at NTH, already publishing in JPT. The collaboration with Øystein Glasø (the same Glasø whose 1980 oil-property correlation is in every PVT software package) is one of his first North-Sea-flavoured publications. The paper is a three-page JPT Forum piece, but the practical conclusion is one of the heuristics that PVT engineers still rely on when they need a quick separator-flash answer without setting up a full EOS.

The question is narrow. When a black-oil sample is flashed through a low-pressure separator using Katz-Hachmuth K-values fit by Standing's 1979 equations, what should be assumed for the heptanes-plus K-value? Standing's framework gives K-values for individual single-carbon-number components up to C6, but a tail K-value for the lumped C7+ pseudocomponent has to be supplied separately. Glasø and Whitson check what happens if you just use a simple rule of thumb.

Key Contribution

  • The 0.15 heuristic. KC7+ = 0.15 · KC7. A practical rule, not a derived correlation. Valid when separator pressure is below 1,000 psia, temperature below 250°F, and the oil contains a substantial heptanes-plus fraction (mole fraction above 0.70) so that minor errors in KC7+ do not propagate to the overall flash result.
  • Validation across 15 crude oils. Middle East, North Sea, California, Michigan, Mississippi, plus the FA-1, GL-1, GL-2 reference fluids. Paraffinicity spans Fp = 11.38 to 12.40. Nonhydrocarbon content reaches 57% CO2, 24% N2, 7% H2S. The flash predictions agree with measured separator products to:
    • GOR within 2.18% on average (standard deviation 4.16%)
    • Gas gravity to -0.35% (SD 5.37%)
    • Stock-tank API gravity to -2.49% (SD 3.50%)
  • The honest caveat. Whitson notes in the paper that a proper calculation of KC7+ "was not available to the authors at the time this study was conducted" and that Standing's 1979 reference contained the method to do it rigorously. The 0.15 rule is the pragmatic engineering choice when the C7+ fraction dominates the mass balance. For lean gases or condensates where C7+ contributes a smaller share, a better-characterised KC7+ is needed.

Significance and Contribution

Characteristic Whitson move. State the simplification openly, quantify when it is safe, document the more rigorous alternative. This pattern, three years before SPE-12233, is already mature: pragmatic engineering with honest validity envelopes rather than universal claims.

Practical legacy. The 0.15 multiplier shows up in field-engineering quick-look calculations, in graduate teaching examples, and in default separator-flash modules of older PVT packages. Engineers who have never read the paper still apply the rule because it works inside its stated envelope.

Knowledge Built Upon

  • Katz & Hachmuth (1937) - the foundational low-pressure K-value correlation set
  • Standing (1979) - the equation-fit form of the Katz-Hachmuth K-values that Whitson is using as the flash engine

What It Contributed To

  • Quick-look engineering separator-flash calculations across the next four decades
  • Teaching examples in PVT courses that pair the simple K-value approach with the rigorous EOS calculation, showing where each is appropriate
  • The 1983 SPE-12233 heptanes-plus characterisation work, where the same author voice ("state when the simple thing is enough") returns at full technical weight

Key Collaborator

Øystein S. Glasø — the author of the Glasø 1980 oil-property correlations (bubblepoint, FVF, viscosity) that remain in every modern PVT software package. At the time of this paper Glasø was at Continental Shelf Institute (IKU) in Trondheim. The Glasø-Whitson 1982 paper is one of the early Norwegian-author collaborations in Curtis's bibliography.
