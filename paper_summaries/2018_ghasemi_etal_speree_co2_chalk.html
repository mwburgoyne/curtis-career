Context

Distinct study from the 2016 SPE-179550 paper. Where the 2016 paper used a static constant-volume diffusion cell with pressure-decline monitoring, this 2018 SPEREE study uses a flow cell with top injection and bottom production. PVT characterisation and EOS model are imported from the 2016 work; new contributions are the apparatus, the paired-experiment design, the time-varying diffusion-coefficient history-match workflow, and the Peclet-number plus viscous-gravity-ratio diagnostic framework. Sigerslev outcrop chalk (Maastrichtian, Tor Formation), 7.37 cm long by 3.76 cm diameter with a 0.6 cm centralised axial hole serving as the "fracture" (matrix porosity 47%, permeability 2.5 md; matrix/fracture pore-volume ratio ~18:1). North Sea reservoir conditions: 258.5 bara / 110 °C for STO experiment, 335.9 bara / 100 °C for n-C10.

Key Contributions

  • Paired STO + n-C10 experimental design isolates the diffusion mechanism via density matching. The novel design choice. The STO experiment runs at 258.5 bara / 110 °C with STO density 0.834 g/cm3 and gas density 0.562 g/cm3 - a 49% density contrast that drives gravity-convection alongside diffusion. The n-C10 experiment runs at 335.9 bara / 100 °C deliberately chosen so n-C10 (0.698 g/cm3) and CO2 (0.699 g/cm3) are density-matched within 0.15% - suppressing gravity-driven convection and forcing the oil-recovery mechanism to be diffusion-dominated. The pair separates the two transport mechanisms that the STO experiment alone could not resolve.
  • Time-varying diffusion coefficient via three sequential models as workaround for the constant-D simulator limitation. The STO experiment runs 24.4 pore volumes over 440 hours, during which the average composition in the chalk evolves from 0% CO2 to 85-90%. Diffusion coefficients are strongly compositional-dependent (per Ghasemi 2016) but ECLIPSE 300 uses a single constant D per component pair, so no single-D simulation matches the full experiment. Workaround: run three sequential models stitched together in Pipe-It - Model 1 for 0-80 hours using D from a 25% CO2 mixture, Model 2 for 80-200 hours using D from 60%, Model 3 for 200-440 hours using D from 90%. The average composition sets the D value used for each interval. Engineers' compromise that gets the right answer despite the simulator limitation.
  • Peclet number plus viscous-gravity ratio diagnostic framework. NPe = uL/Kl with Kl = D + αu per Perkins-Johnston 1963, evaluated per grid cell post-simulation, gives matrix NPe ≈ 0.001 (STO) and 0.002 (n-C10); fracture NPe = 0.08-1. Kl/D ≈ 1.005 for the matrix confirms diffusion dominates over physical dispersion. Viscous-gravity ratio Rv/g = uμo/(kgΔρ) = 0.21 per Wylie-Mohanty 1999 shows gravity is 5x stronger than viscous force, explaining the 15% oil recovery observed in a control no-diffusion case (pure gravity drainage of fracture oil). The vertical-permeability sensitivity (kv = 0 vs 2.6 md) gives near-identical recovery curves, confirming negligible vertical convection. The combined diagnostic distinguishes diffusion-dominated from gravity-dominated and convection-dominated mechanisms.
  • Capillary pressure dominance differentiates STO (20% recovery swing) from n-C10 (negligible effect). In the STO experiment, capillary pressure controls a clean front-displacement pattern: CO2 cannot enter the top of the matrix because Pcgo (Christoffersen 1992 data scaled to IFT 1.5 mN/m) exceeds the overburden head. Instead CO2 enters from below via capillary suction, displacing oil from the top down. The Pc = 0 control case gives 82% oil recovery after 200 hours, 20 percentage points higher than the base case (62%), because CO2 can then enter the top of the matrix immediately and vaporise light-to-medium components efficiently. In the n-C10 experiment, CO2 and n-C10 are first-contact miscible at experimental conditions, so capillary forces have no effect (no distinct phases to separate) and Pc sensitivity is negligible.

Significance and Contribution

The density-matched n-C10 trick. Curtis's teaching pattern: when a laboratory experiment confounds three mechanisms (diffusion, gravity-convection, capillary), engineer a complementary experiment where two of them are guaranteed inactive. The density-matched n-C10 pair gives diffusion alone, which back-solves the diffusion coefficient cleanly. The STO companion gives the full mechanism cocktail, whose recovery curves can then be decomposed using the back-solved D. The paired design is more informative than two independent experiments.

Honest compromise on simulator limitations. ECLIPSE 300's constant-D limitation is not solved in this paper - it is worked around with three-model stitching. The authors are explicit about the limitation and explicit about the workaround. This is the right professional discipline: don't tune around the problem and hide it; report the workaround as the workaround and flag the underlying limitation for next-generation simulator development.

The diagnostic framework travels. Peclet number plus viscous-gravity ratio plus capillary-pressure sensitivity together classify the dominant transport mechanism. This three-number framework is portable to other fractured-reservoir recovery analyses including waterflood, gas injection, and underground hydrogen storage. The methodology Whitson's group developed for chalk CO2-EOR ends up shaping later workflows in different fluid contexts.
