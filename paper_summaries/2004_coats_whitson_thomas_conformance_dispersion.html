Modeling Conformance as Dispersion

Context

Keith H. Coats wrote SENSOR. He had been one of the most influential reservoir-simulation engineers of his generation, with a track record running from the 1960s Mobil thermal-simulator work, through the early commercial compositional simulators of the 1980s, to Coats Engineering's SENSOR in the 1990s. Whitson had been using SENSOR for compositional work for years and knew Coats's contributions at depth. The 2004 SPE-90390 paper is the only Coats-Whitson first-author collaboration in either of their corpora. Larry K. Thomas of ConocoPhillips is the third author. The seniority pairing matters: this is two of the field's most senior reservoir-simulation engineers, of comparable standing, writing together. Whitson is 48; Coats is in his late sixties.

The technical target is a long-standing literature confusion. Field tracer tests routinely yield apparent dispersivities of 100 to 1000+ feet by 1-D convection-dispersion fits, while lab corefloods give 0.01 ft. Many authors had concluded dispersivity is scale-dependent, with a physical mechanism that scales up with travel distance. Coats-Whitson-Thomas argue this is a mistake. The large apparent dispersivities reflect conformance (heterogeneity + well pattern + completion + drift), not physical dispersion. Physical dispersivity is a rock property near 0.01 ft, independent of scale. The two should not be conflated in simulator inputs.

Key Contributions

  • Physical vs apparent dispersivity - definitions that differ by 3-5 orders of magnitude. Physical dispersivity α is a microstructural rock property measured by lab corefloods; for consolidated rocks α ≈ 0.01 ft, for unconsolidated sandpacks an order of magnitude smaller. Scale-invariant and time-invariant. Apparent dispersivity αa is the best-fit value of the 1-D convection-dispersion equation matched to effluent profiles from field tracer tests or numerical simulations. Field-scale αa is typically 100 to >1000 ft, i.e. 104 to 105 times the physical value. Decomposition: αa ≈ α + αac where αac is the conformance contribution, and αac ≫ α in nearly all field cases, so αa ≈ αac is a working approximation.
  • Heterogeneity alone causes no in-situ mixing. For the transport equation with longitudinal dispersivity α = 0, transverse αt = 0, and molecular diffusion D0 = 0, the equation reduces to a first-order hyperbolic form with no second-order diffusive terms. No mixing occurs anywhere in the reservoir, however complex the velocity field. Heterogeneity affects the velocity field and the front shape, but cannot introduce mixing without a diffusive second derivative. A 2-D heterogeneous five-spot simulated with 3DSL streamline code (567×567 grid, 3688 streamlines, QD = 0.3366 PV) shows concentrations of exactly 0 or 1 everywhere - no intermediate values. The effluent profile is smeared (apparent Peclet number 2.7, apparent dispersivity 7 times the homogeneous baseline) but the smearing is in the production weighting of streamlines, not in the reservoir.
  • Additive dispersivity decomposition - αa ≈ α + αap + αas. Apparent dispersivity decomposes approximately additively into physical dispersion, areal (pattern) conformance, and stratification (vertical) conformance. The Peclet-inverse form 1/NPea ≈ 1/NPe + 1/NPeap + 1/NPeas is exact for zero stratification (V = 0) and accurate within a few percent for V up to 0.4. A worked verification: stratified five-spot with Dykstra-Parsons V = 0.13 and α = 0.01 ft gives direct-fit 1/NPea = 0.01977 vs the decomposition prediction 0.01974 - agreement within 1%.
  • Scale dependence of αa is an algebraic artefact, not a physical mechanism. The widely-reported slope-of-unity behaviour on log-log plots of αa vs travel distance L is a necessary consequence of fitting the single-parameter 1-D CD equation (parameter Peclet number L/α) to profiles reflecting conformance. Similar systems (same heterogeneity, same well pattern) have identical C(QD) profiles by construction. The 1-D CD fit gives the same NPea, which by αa = L/NPea forces αa ∝ L. Slope unity is algebraically forced, not evidence of a physical mechanism. Under drift in single-well echo tests the slope can take any value from -∞ to +∞ depending on the specific combination of injection rate and time used to vary L.
  • Use physical dispersivity, not apparent, as simulator input. The practical recommendation. Reservoir-process simulations - miscible solvent floods, enriched-gas drives above MME, tracer tests, bank breakdown, in-situ scale precipitation, chemical reaction - should use α ≈ 0.01 ft as the CD transport coefficient. Transverse αt ≈ α/10 to α/30 per Perkins-Johnston (1963) for sandstones. Conformance should be modelled explicitly via fine-scale permeability distribution, well placement, and drift boundary conditions. Apparent-dispersivity values are appropriate only as correction terms in coarse-grid or reduced-dimension history-matching models; never use the history-matched value (≫ 0.01 ft) in the physical CD flow terms describing miscibility, scale, or reaction. Using 100-1000 ft input dispersivities (as in Solano et al. 2001 and Stalkup 1998 studies of enriched-gas drives) forces in-situ mixing that does not physically occur, breaks miscibility banks artificially, and overstates the pessimistic effects of dispersion on recovery.
  • Numerical demonstration across simulator codes. The paper exercises Sensor (Coats Engineering), 3DSL (Streamline), and UTCHEM finite-difference codes against analytical Muskat 1949 stratification solutions and Pickens-Grisak 1981 field echo tests. The streamline code provides the visual proof of the no-mixing result. Sensor and UTCHEM with α = 0 reproduce the analytical stratification profiles within numerical-dispersion accuracy. The cross-simulator agreement is the structural argument that the result is physics, not implementation.

Significance and Contribution

The Coats-Whitson collaboration. SPE-90390 is the only first-author Coats-Whitson paper in either corpus. Keith Coats was the founder of Coats Engineering and the author of SENSOR, the compositional reservoir simulator that runs alongside Eclipse and CMG in commercial use. He was one of the most influential reservoir-simulation engineers of his generation, with a publication record running from the 1960s Mobil thermal work through the early commercial compositional simulators of the 1980s. The 2004 paper is the seniority pairing - two senior figures of comparable standing, writing about a result they both believed was being misused industry-wide. The collaboration continued through the 2006 SPE Handbook compositional-simulation chapter (Coats, Thomas, Pierson - with Whitson credited).

Literature correction. Decades of papers had presented αa vs L log-log plots as evidence for scale-dependent physical dispersion. Coats-Whitson-Thomas re-interpret the same plots as evidence for scale-dependent conformance combined with scale-invariant physical dispersion. The two are routinely conflated. The 2004 paper is the clearest published statement of the distinction and the recommended remediation in simulator input choice. The argument is strongly worded: Conclusion 4 calls the observed scale dependence of apparent dispersivity "empty of meaning" when used as evidence of physical mechanism.

Impact on miscible-flood economics. Studies that used 100-1000 ft input dispersivities to predict MME-enrichment trade-offs (Solano-Johns-Lake 2001), bank breakdown (Stalkup 1998), and enriched-gas-drive recovery (Johns et al. 2000) systematically overstated the pessimistic effects of dispersion. The 2004 paper argues these studies should be re-run with α = 0.01 ft and explicit conformance modelling. The trade-offs shift in favour of more miscible recovery when the input is physical rather than apparent. Quantifying the shift is left as further work, but the methodological correction is what the paper insists on.

Phase 3 maturity. SPE-90390 belongs to the productivity peak of Whitson's career. The 2000 Phase Behavior monograph is recent; the Fevang-Whitson-Sævareid TIPM consolidation came out in 2003; the Singh-Whitson BO PVT papers are running; the Alavian-Whitson CO2 EOR series is starting in 2005 with IPTC-10641. The Coats collaboration is the methodological side-project that says: the framework of physical vs apparent dispersivity has to be straight before any of the rest of the compositional-simulation edifice can be relied on.

  • Pickens & Grisak (1981) — the canonical field echo tests with apparent dispersivities orders of magnitude above lab values

What It Contributed To

  • Coats, Thomas & Pierson (2006) SPE Handbook, compositional-simulation chapter — carries the physical-vs-apparent dispersivity guidance into the textbook reference
  • Subsequent miscible-flood and tracer-test simulation studies that use α ≈ 0.01 ft as the physical input and resolve conformance via fine-grid permeability fields
  • Coarse-grid history-matching workflows that treat apparent dispersivity as a correction term confined to the reduced model, not exported into physical flow terms
  • Streamline-based reservoir engineering practice that uses 3DSL-style codes to diagnose conformance contributions to effluent profiles separately from physical mixing
  • Re-evaluation of enriched-gas-drive recovery predictions that had used apparent-dispersivity inputs
  • The 2009 SPE TWA "Pillars of Technology" piece by Whitson revisits the dispersion-vs-conformance lesson among the broader reservoir-engineering pillars
